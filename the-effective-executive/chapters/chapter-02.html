Chapter 2: Know Thy Time

Practice One

Time is the scarcest resource. Unlike money or people, you can't rent, hire, or buy more of it. Effective executives start not with tasks but with time—they know where their time goes, and they manage it systematically.

Time Is the Limiting Factor

Everything requires time. And time is totally irreplaceable. You can substitute other resources, but not time.

"Time is the scarcest resource, and unless it is managed, nothing else can be managed."
— Peter Drucker

Most people are poor judges of their own time usage. What they think they do and what they actually do rarely match.

The Three-Step Process

Drucker prescribes a three-step process for managing time:

Step 1: Record Time

Track how you actually spend your time—not how you think you spend it. Keep a log for several weeks. Most people are shocked by the results.

Step 2: Manage Time

Analyze the log and cut out time-wasters. Ask of every activity: "What would happen if this were not done at all?" If the answer is "nothing," stop doing it.

Step 3: Consolidate Time

Block remaining discretionary time into large, continuous chunks. Effective work requires concentrated time—not fragments scattered throughout the day.

Time-Wasters to Eliminate

Drucker identifies common time-wasters that can be eliminated or reduced:

Systemic Time-Wasters

  • Recurrent crises: If the same crisis happens repeatedly, it's a system failure—fix the system
  • Overstaffing: Too many people spend time coordinating rather than working
  • Malorganization: Too many meetings signal poor organizational design
  • Information malfunction: Information not reaching the right people

The Diagnostic Questions

For each activity in your time log, ask:

Time Audit Questions

  1. "What would happen if this were not done at all?" (If nothing, eliminate it)
  2. "Which of these activities could be done by someone else just as well?" (Delegate)
  3. "What do I do that wastes your time?" (Ask your subordinates)

Consolidating Discretionary Time

After eliminating waste, consolidate what remains. Important work requires concentrated time.

The Power of Blocks

Writing a report takes 6-8 concentrated hours. But those hours must be continuous—not 15 minutes here and 30 there. Effective executives protect blocks of time and fight fragmentation.

Fragmented vs. Consolidated Time

Fragmented
  • 30 minutes between meetings
  • Constant interruptions
  • Reactive mode
  • Shallow work only
Consolidated
  • 3-4 hour blocks
  • Protected from interruption
  • Proactive mode
  • Deep work possible

Key Takeaways

  • Time is the scarcest resource and cannot be replaced
  • Record actual time use—most people misjudge their own time
  • Eliminate activities that produce no results if abandoned
  • Delegate what others could do equally well
  • Consolidate discretionary time into large blocks for important work
  • Recurrent crises indicate system failure—fix the system

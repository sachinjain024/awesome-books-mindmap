Chapter 6: Test

Part Two: Steer

This chapter introduces the Minimum Viable Product (MVP)—perhaps the most famous concept from the Lean Startup methodology. The MVP is the fastest way to get through the Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop with the minimum amount of effort. It's not about building a minimal product; it's about maximizing learning with minimal resources.

What Is a Minimum Viable Product?

The MVP is often misunderstood. It's not about building a crappy product. It's about testing your most critical assumptions with the least amount of effort.

Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
The version of a new product that allows a team to collect the maximum amount of validated learning about customers with the least effort. The goal is not to build a minimal product, but to start the learning process as quickly as possible.
"The MVP is that version of the product that enables a full turn of the Build-Measure-Learn loop with a minimum amount of effort and the least amount of development time."
— Eric Ries

Types of MVPs

MVPs take many forms depending on what you need to learn. Here are the most common types:

Video MVP

Dropbox created a simple video demonstrating their product concept before building it. The video drove signups from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight—validating demand before writing much code.

Concierge MVP

Deliver the service manually to a small number of customers before automating. Food on the Table started by having an employee personally interview customers about their preferences and create custom meal plans by hand.

Wizard of Oz MVP

The product appears automated to customers but is actually powered by humans behind the scenes (like Zappos's early shoe photos). Test the customer experience before building the technology.

Landing Page MVP

A simple web page describing your product with a signup form. Measure interest before building anything. If people won't sign up for a product that doesn't exist, they probably won't use one that does.

The Dropbox Story

Dropbox is one of the most famous MVP success stories.

Case Study: Dropbox Video MVP

Dropbox faced a challenge: they couldn't easily demonstrate their file-syncing technology without building it. Their solution was a three-minute video showing the product concept. Key insights:

  • The video was aimed at early adopters who would "get it"
  • Signups jumped from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight
  • They validated demand before investing heavily in engineering
  • The video cost almost nothing compared to building the product

Overcoming MVP Objections

Entrepreneurs often resist building MVPs. Ries addresses common objections:

Objection: "Customers will judge us by this low-quality product"

Response: Early adopters expect rough products. They're more forgiving than mainstream customers and will provide valuable feedback. If your MVP is too polished, you've likely invested too much before learning.

Objection: "Competitors will steal our idea"

Response: The biggest risk is not that competitors will steal your idea—it's that you'll build something nobody wants. Speed of learning beats secrecy. Most competitors are too busy with their own problems to copy yours.

Objection: "Our brand will be damaged"

Response: Launch under a different name if needed, but don't delay learning. A bad MVP that teaches you something is better than a polished product that nobody uses.

The Quality Paradox

There's an apparent contradiction in the MVP concept: if you build something "minimum," how can it be good enough for customers to use?

Key Insight

Quality in an MVP means quality of learning, not quality of features. A well-designed experiment that teaches you something valuable is higher quality than a polished product that teaches you nothing.

The MVP should have just enough quality to attract early adopters and generate valid learning. The definition of "enough" depends on your customers and what you need to learn.

When to Build an MVP

The answer is almost always "now." Entrepreneurs consistently overestimate how much they need to build before they can learn from customers.

MVP Decision Process

  1. Identify your leap-of-faith assumptions
  2. Ask: What's the simplest way to test these assumptions?
  3. Build only what's necessary to run that test
  4. Run the experiment and measure results
  5. Learn and iterate
"If we do not know who the customer is, we do not know what quality is."
— Eric Ries

Key Takeaways

  • The MVP is about learning, not about building a minimal product
  • MVPs can take many forms: video, concierge, wizard of oz, landing page, and more
  • Early adopters are forgiving of rough products if they solve a real problem
  • The risk of building too much before learning is greater than the risk of launching too early
  • Quality means quality of learning, not just quality of features
  • Competitors stealing your idea is rarely the real risk—building something nobody wants is
  • Build only what's necessary to test your most critical assumptions

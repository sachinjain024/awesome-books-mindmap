Chapter 7: Measure

Part Two: Steer

How do you measure progress when you don't know what will work? Traditional accounting doesn't work for startups—you can't measure ROI on experiments or calculate the NPV of validated learning. This chapter introduces Innovation Accounting: a new way to measure whether your startup is making real progress.

Why Traditional Metrics Fail

Standard business metrics assume a stable, predictable environment. Revenue, profit margins, and market share matter when you know your business model works. But startups don't know if their model works—that's what they're trying to learn.

The Danger of Vanity Metrics

Vanity metrics make you feel good but don't help you make decisions. Total users, page views, and raw revenue can all go up while your business is actually failing. They tell you what happened but not why or what to do about it.

A startup needs metrics that help it decide what to do next. Should we pivot or persevere? Is this feature worth the investment? Are we getting closer to product-market fit?

Innovation Accounting

Innovation accounting is a three-step process for measuring startup progress:

The Three Steps of Innovation Accounting

  1. Establish the baseline: Use an MVP to establish real data on where the company stands right now
  2. Tune the engine: Make changes and experiments to move the metrics from the baseline toward the ideal
  3. Pivot or persevere: Evaluate whether you're making sufficient progress toward the ideal, or need to change direction

This creates a clear framework for measuring progress: start with current reality, set a target, run experiments to improve metrics, and make a decision based on results.

Actionable vs. Vanity Metrics

The key distinction is between metrics that help you make decisions (actionable) and metrics that just make you feel good (vanity).

Vanity Metrics Actionable Metrics
Total registered users Active users per week
Total page views Conversion rate from visit to signup
Total revenue (cumulative) Revenue per customer
Number of downloads Retention rate after 30 days
Press mentions Customer acquisition cost

Key Insight

A good metric is one that helps you decide what to do next. If a metric goes up or down and you wouldn't change your actions, it's probably a vanity metric.

Cohort Analysis

One of the most powerful tools for actionable metrics is cohort analysis: grouping customers by when they joined and tracking their behavior over time.

How Cohort Analysis Works

Instead of looking at total users, segment users by when they signed up:

  • January cohort: Users who joined in January
  • February cohort: Users who joined in February
  • Etc.

Then track each cohort's behavior: retention, conversion, revenue. This reveals whether your product is improving for new users or if vanity metrics are masking problems.

Case Study: Grockit's Cohort Analysis

Grockit, an online learning company, used cohort analysis to discover that their "improvements" weren't actually helping. New features increased total usage but didn't improve retention rates for new cohorts. This led them to rethink their approach entirely.

Split Testing (A/B Testing)

Split testing is essential for understanding cause and effect. Without it, you don't know if changes in metrics are due to your actions or external factors.

Running a Split Test

  1. Formulate a clear hypothesis about what will improve metrics
  2. Create two versions: control (current) and treatment (new)
  3. Randomly assign users to each version
  4. Measure the difference in key metrics
  5. Make decisions based on statistical significance
"The only way to know why customers behave the way they do is to observe them directly, side by side, in controlled experiments."
— Eric Ries

The Three A's of Metrics

Good metrics should be:

Actionable
Demonstrates clear cause and effect. You know what action caused the change.
Accessible
Simple enough that everyone understands them. Use concrete, tangible units.
Auditable
You can verify the data is real. Access to underlying raw data, not just dashboards.

Key Takeaways

  • Vanity metrics feel good but don't help you make decisions
  • Innovation accounting: establish baseline, tune the engine, pivot or persevere
  • Actionable metrics demonstrate clear cause and effect
  • Cohort analysis reveals whether your product is truly improving
  • Split testing is essential for understanding what actually works
  • Good metrics are actionable, accessible, and auditable

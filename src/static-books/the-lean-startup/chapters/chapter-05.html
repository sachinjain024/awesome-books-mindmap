Chapter 5: Leap

Part Two: Steer

Every business plan contains assumptions—guesses about what will make the business work. This chapter introduces the concept of leap-of-faith assumptions: the most critical hypotheses that determine whether your startup will succeed or fail. Identifying and testing these assumptions is essential before investing heavily.

Leap-of-Faith Assumptions

Every startup's business plan contains assumptions that are taken on faith. These aren't minor details—they're fundamental beliefs about how the world works that underlie the entire venture.

Leap-of-Faith Assumptions
The riskiest elements of a startup's plan—the things that must be true for the business to work but that haven't been proven yet. They're called "leap of faith" because entrepreneurs bet everything on them.

The danger is that entrepreneurs often don't recognize their assumptions as assumptions. They treat beliefs as facts and build elaborate plans on unverified foundations.

The Two Most Critical Assumptions

While every business has many assumptions, Ries identifies two that are almost always the most important:

The Value Hypothesis

Tests whether a product or service really delivers value to customers once they are using it.

The Growth Hypothesis

Tests how new customers will discover a product or service. What's the mechanism for growth?

These two hypotheses are interrelated but distinct. A product can deliver value but have no growth mechanism, or grow rapidly but deliver little value (which leads to churn).

Facebook's Leap of Faith

Ries uses Facebook as an example of identifying and testing leap-of-faith assumptions.

Case Study: Facebook's Early Metrics

Facebook tracked whether users came back to the site day after day (value hypothesis) and whether they invited friends (growth hypothesis). Early data showed:

  • More than half of users came back every single day
  • At campuses where Facebook launched, the majority of students signed up within weeks

These weren't vanity metrics—they demonstrated that the value and growth hypotheses were valid.

Key Insight

Facebook didn't need millions of users to validate their assumptions. The pattern of engagement and growth at individual campuses was enough to demonstrate the model worked.

Analogs and Antilogs

When identifying assumptions, look at what has worked before (analogs) and what has failed (antilogs). This helps clarify which assumptions are truly risky.

Using Analogs and Antilogs

Analog: A comparable company or product that proved something works. iPod's success showed people would pay for portable digital music (analog for iTunes).

Antilog: A failure that shows what doesn't work. Napster's legal troubles showed that illegal music distribution wasn't sustainable (antilog for iTunes).

Apple's iPod/iTunes strategy was built on learning from both: people want digital music (analog) but it needs to be legal (antilog).

Genchi Gembutsu

Ries borrows a concept from Toyota's manufacturing philosophy: genchi gembutsu, which translates to "go and see for yourself."

"You cannot be sure you really understand any part of any business problem unless you go and see for yourself firsthand."
— Toyota Principle

For startups, this means getting out of the building and observing real customers in their natural environment. You can't understand customer problems from behind a desk or through surveys alone.

Applying Genchi Gembutsu

  1. Identify who your customer might be
  2. Go to where they are (home, office, store)
  3. Observe their behavior without interrupting
  4. Ask open-ended questions about their problems
  5. Look for behaviors that contradict what they say

Getting Out of the Building

Steve Blank, one of the pioneers of the Lean Startup movement, famously said "Get out of the building." This complements genchi gembutsu.

The Danger of Building in Isolation

Many entrepreneurs prefer building products to talking to customers. Building feels productive; customer conversations feel uncomfortable. But building without customer contact is the fastest path to building something nobody wants.

Customer development should happen in parallel with product development, not as an afterthought once the product is "ready."

Key Takeaways

  • Leap-of-faith assumptions are the riskiest elements of your plan that must be true for success
  • The value hypothesis tests whether customers get value; the growth hypothesis tests how you'll acquire customers
  • Use analogs (what worked) and antilogs (what failed) to clarify your assumptions
  • Practice genchi gembutsu—go and see for yourself how customers behave
  • Get out of the building early and often to understand real customer needs

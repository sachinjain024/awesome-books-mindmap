Chapter 8: Pivot (or Persevere)

Part Two: Steer

The pivot is one of the most critical—and most misunderstood—concepts in the Lean Startup. It's not about failing or giving up. It's a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about your product, strategy, or engine of growth. This chapter explores when and how to pivot.

What Is a Pivot?

A pivot is not a random change or a desperate attempt to save a failing company. It's a strategic decision based on validated learning.

Pivot
A structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, business model, or engine of growth. It keeps one foot grounded in what has been learned while changing direction.
"A pivot is a change in strategy without a change in vision."
— Eric Ries

The vision remains constant—you still believe you can create value and build a sustainable business. But the strategy for getting there changes based on what you've learned.

The Pivot or Persevere Meeting

Ries recommends scheduling regular "pivot or persevere" meetings—perhaps monthly—to force a structured evaluation of progress.

Pivot or Persevere Meeting Agenda

  1. Review innovation accounting metrics since the last meeting
  2. Assess progress toward the business model goals
  3. Share customer insights and feedback
  4. Discuss whether current strategy is working
  5. Make a decision: pivot, persevere, or need more data

Avoiding the "Land of the Living Dead"

Without regular pivot/persevere decisions, startups drift into the "land of the living dead"—not failing dramatically, but not succeeding either. They consume resources without making progress. Regular decision points prevent this fate.

Types of Pivots

Pivots come in many forms. Here are the most common:

Zoom-in Pivot

What was previously considered a single feature becomes the whole product. A feature that customers love gets spun out as the entire focus.

Zoom-out Pivot

The opposite: what was the whole product becomes a single feature of a larger product. The original vision was too narrow.

Customer Segment Pivot

The product solves a real problem, but for a different customer segment than originally planned. Keep the product, change the target market.

Customer Need Pivot

Through customer discovery, you learn that the problem you're solving isn't important enough. But you've learned about a related problem that is. Keep the customer, solve a different problem.

Platform Pivot

A change from an application to a platform or vice versa. What started as a single app becomes a platform for others to build on.

Business Architecture Pivot

Switching from high margin, low volume (complex systems) to low margin, high volume (volume operations) or vice versa.

Value Capture Pivot

Changes to how the company captures value (monetization). Moving from subscription to freemium, or from advertising to direct sales.

Engine of Growth Pivot

Changing the primary mechanism for growth: from viral to paid, from paid to sticky, etc. (More on engines of growth in Chapter 10.)

Channel Pivot

Changing how you deliver the product to customers: from direct sales to retail, from web to mobile, from online to in-person.

Technology Pivot

Using a completely different technology to deliver the same solution. The customer problem and value proposition remain the same.

Wealthfront's Pivot Story

Ries shares the story of Wealthfront (originally called KaChing) as an example of successful pivoting.

Case Study: Wealthfront

Wealthfront originally launched as a social platform for amateur investors to share their stock picks. The idea was that anyone could become a fund manager. But the team discovered:

  • Amateur investors didn't want the responsibility of managing others' money
  • Professional money managers were more interested in the platform
  • Customers wanted automated investing, not social features

The pivot: from a social platform for amateur investors to an automated investment service (robo-advisor). Same vision of democratizing wealth management, completely different strategy.

The Courage to Pivot

Pivoting requires courage. It means admitting that your original hypothesis was wrong—and that's psychologically difficult.

Key Insight

Every successful startup has had to pivot. The ability to pivot is not a sign of failure—it's a sign of learning. Companies that can't pivot are the ones that fail.

Common reasons companies resist pivoting:

  • Vanity metrics make progress look better than it is
  • Sunk cost fallacy: "We've invested so much already"
  • Fear: What if the next idea is worse?
  • Ego: Admitting the original vision was wrong feels like failure
"The sign of a successful pivot is that you're more productive after the pivot than before."
— Eric Ries

Key Takeaways

  • A pivot is a structured course correction based on validated learning
  • It's a change in strategy, not a change in vision
  • Schedule regular "pivot or persevere" meetings to force decisions
  • There are many types of pivots—understand your options
  • Without pivoting, startups drift into the "land of the living dead"
  • Pivoting requires courage but is a sign of learning, not failure
  • Vanity metrics and sunk costs are the enemies of good pivot decisions
  • Every successful startup has pivoted at least once

Chapter 6: Written Communication

Part Two: Execution

Spoken communication doesn't scale. As the company grows, you can't repeat the same message in every meeting. Writing scales: one memo or update reaches everyone, can be referenced later, and forces clarity of thought. This chapter covers why and how to use written communication as a core operating tool.

Why Writing Scales

When you say something in a meeting, only the people in the room hear it—and they may remember it differently. When you write it down, everyone can read it, anytime. Writing also forces you to think clearly: fuzzy ideas don't survive the transition to sentences. For strategy, decisions, and context, writing is the leverage that lets you scale your communication.

Key Insight

If you have to say the same thing in 10 meetings, you should have written it once and shared it. Use writing for anything that needs to reach more than a few people or persist as reference.

What to Write

Common written artifacts: Strategy memos (where we're going and why), decision memos (what we decided and the reasoning), weekly or monthly updates (progress, metrics, priorities), and post-mortems or learnings (what happened and what we'll do differently). Not everything needs a long doc—sometimes a short update in Slack or email is enough. Match the format to the audience and the stakes.

When to Write vs. Meet

  • Write: Strategy, decisions, context, updates that many people need
  • Meet: Discussion, debate, feedback, relationship-building, complex decisions that need back-and-forth

Updates: Cadence and Content

Regular updates (e.g., weekly from the CEO or each team lead) keep the company aligned. Include: key metrics, wins, challenges, and priorities. Keep them scannable: bullets, short paragraphs, clear headers. Consistency matters—if you skip weeks, people stop reading. Share in a central place (email, Slack, Notion, etc.) so everyone knows where to find them.

Weekly Update Structure (example)

  • Metrics: 2–5 key numbers and trend
  • Wins: What went well
  • Challenges: What's hard or at risk
  • Priorities next week: What we're focusing on

Clarity and Brevity

Good writing is clear and concise. Lead with the main point. Use short sentences and paragraphs. Avoid jargon unless the audience shares it. If a section doesn't add value, cut it. The goal is to be understood quickly—respect the reader's time.

"Write so that the busiest person in the company can understand you in one pass. If they have to read it twice, you didn't do your job."
— Matt Mochary

Common Mistake

Relying on meetings to communicate important decisions or strategy, then wondering why people are misaligned. Write the strategy and key decisions down; use meetings to discuss and refine, not to replace the document.

Key Takeaways

  • Writing scales; speaking doesn't. Use memos and updates for strategy, decisions, and context.
  • Write strategy memos, decision memos, and regular updates (weekly/monthly) so everyone has a single source of truth.
  • Keep updates scannable: metrics, wins, challenges, priorities.
  • Be clear and concise; lead with the main point and respect the reader's time.

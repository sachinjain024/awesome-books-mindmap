Chapter 5: Understand What Motivates Each Person

Part 3: Understand What Motivates Each Person

Not everyone wants to climb the corporate ladder. Some people want rapid career growth; others want to excel at what they do without taking on management. Both paths are valid, and great bosses understand what each person on their team wants.

The Career Ladder Myth

Organizations assume everyone wants to move "up." But for many people, moving into management means doing less of what they love.

The Problem with "Up or Out"

  • Loses great individual contributors by forcing them into management
  • Creates bad managers (people who don't want to manage)
  • Punishes people who want to stay excellent at their craft
  • Assumes ambition only means climbing the ladder
  • Ignores people's actual motivations and strengths

Steep Growth Trajectory vs. Gradual Growth Trajectory

Kim Scott proposes two valid career trajectories: people on a steep growth trajectory (ambitious for new challenges and promotions) and people on a gradual growth trajectory (ambitious for excellence in their current role).

Two Valid Paths

Steep Growth Trajectory

Characteristics:

  • Wants rapid career progression
  • Seeks new challenges constantly
  • Willing to change roles, teams, or companies
  • Energized by learning new things
  • Thrives on increased responsibility

Your role as boss: Give stretch assignments, promotions, new challenges

Gradual Growth Trajectory

Characteristics:

  • Wants to excel at current role
  • Values stability and mastery
  • Happy without constant change
  • May have priorities outside work (family, hobbies)
  • Not less ambitious—differently ambitious

Your role as boss: Honor their choice, give opportunities for mastery, protect from "up or out" pressure

Trajectories Change

People move between trajectories throughout their careers. Someone might be on a steep trajectory, then shift to gradual after having kids—then shift back years later. The key is staying in conversation.

Life Changes Career Trajectories

Events that might shift someone's trajectory:

  • Having children or caring for aging parents
  • Dealing with health issues
  • Pursuing education or a passion project
  • Recovering from burnout
  • Changing personal priorities
  • Achieving a long-term goal

Your job isn't to judge these choices—it's to understand them and adapt.

"Part of your job as a boss is to put people in roles where they can grow, whether they want to grow into management or deeper into a technical or creative area."
— Kim Scott

Avoiding the "Rockstar" and "Superstar" Labels

Scott initially used "rockstar" (gradual growth) and "superstar" (steep growth) terminology but later regretted it. Labels feel judgmental and miss the point: both paths are equally valuable.

Don't Label People

Instead of labeling people, have conversations:

  • "What do you want to learn this year?"
  • "Are you happy in your current role?"
  • "What would growth look like for you?"
  • "What are your priorities outside of work right now?"
  • "How can I support you in what you want to achieve?"

Building a Balanced Team

Great teams need both types of people. If everyone is on a steep trajectory, you'll have constant turnover. If everyone is on a gradual trajectory, you won't have enough leaders developing.

The Balanced Team

A healthy team has:

  • People on steep trajectories: Bring energy, drive change, take on new initiatives
  • People on gradual trajectories: Bring stability, deep expertise, institutional knowledge
  • Mutual respect: Neither path is seen as "better"
  • Flexibility: People can shift trajectories as life changes

Key Takeaways

  • Not everyone wants to climb the ladder—and that's okay
  • Steep growth trajectory: ambitious for promotion and new challenges
  • Gradual growth trajectory: ambitious for excellence in current role
  • Both trajectories are equally valid and valuable
  • People shift between trajectories as life circumstances change
  • Great teams need both types of people

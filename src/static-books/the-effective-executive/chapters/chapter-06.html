Chapter 6: The Elements of Decision-Making

Practice Five - Part 1

Effective executives make effective decisions. This chapter introduces the systematic approach to decision-making. Drucker emphasizes that effective decisions are based on principles and the dissent of opinions—not on consensus or expedience.

Strategic vs. Tactical Decisions

Effective executives distinguish between decisions that matter and those that don't. They focus their decision-making energy on a few important strategic decisions.

Types of Decisions

Strategic (Generic): Decisions based on principle that apply broadly

Tactical (Unique): Decisions specific to particular situations

Most problems are generic—they've happened before and will happen again. Treat them as such.

"A decision is a judgment. It is a choice between alternatives. It is rarely a choice between right and wrong. It is at best a choice between 'almost right' and 'probably wrong.'"
— Peter Drucker

The Five Elements of Decision-Making

Drucker identifies five essential elements in the decision process:

Element 1: Classify the Problem

Is this generic or unique? Most problems are generic—recurring situations that require a principle or policy. Only truly exceptional events require unique solutions.

Element 2: Define the Specifications

What does the decision need to accomplish? What are the objectives, the minimum goals, and the conditions that must be satisfied? Be clear about what "success" looks like.

Element 3: Start with the Right Thing

Start with what is right, not what is acceptable. Compromise may be necessary later, but start with the ideal solution. Otherwise, you compromise on the compromise.

Element 4: Build in Action

Converting the decision into action is a separate step. Who will carry it out? What do they need to know? What must change? Build action into the decision from the start.

Element 5: Build in Feedback

How will you know if the decision is working? Build in systematic feedback mechanisms. Test assumptions against reality. Go and see for yourself.

The Danger of Plausible Assumptions

Decisions always rest on assumptions about the future. The danger lies in assumptions that seem plausible but are untested.

Testing Assumptions

  • What does this decision assume about the future?
  • How can we test these assumptions?
  • What would tell us we're wrong?
  • How will we know when to revisit this decision?

Boundary Conditions

Every decision has boundary conditions—the specifications it must satisfy. A decision that fails to meet its boundary conditions is worse than no decision.

Setting Boundaries

What are the minimum objectives? What must this decision accomplish? What are the time limits? The resource constraints? The risks we cannot accept?

Clarity about boundaries prevents later disappointment.

Generic vs. Unique Problems

Problem Classification

Generic Problems
  • Recurring situations
  • Require a rule/policy
  • Solve once, apply many times
  • Example: Inventory management
Unique Problems
  • Truly exceptional events
  • Require specific solutions
  • Cannot generalize
  • Example: Merger decision

The common error: treating a generic problem as if it were unique, requiring constant firefighting. Or treating a unique event as generic, applying an inappropriate rule.

Key Takeaways

  • Classify problems: generic (need a principle) or unique (need specific solution)
  • Define specifications: what must the decision accomplish?
  • Start with what's right, not what's acceptable
  • Build action into the decision: who does what by when
  • Build in feedback: how will you know if it's working?
  • Test your assumptions—don't accept plausibility

> "Greatness and nearsightedness are incompatible. Meaningful achievement depends on lifting one's sights and pushing toward the horizon."
> -- Daniel H. Pink

Pink concludes Drive with a comprehensive toolkit for putting the principles of Motivation 3.0 into practice. This chapter is organized around specific, actionable strategies for individuals, organizations, parents, and educators. It is the bridge between understanding motivation and actually changing behavior.

## For Individuals: Nine Strategies for Awakening Your Motivation

### Strategy 1: Give Yourself a Flow Test {.practice-exercise}

Set a reminder on your phone to go off at 40 random times during the week. Each time it rings, write down what you are doing, how you are feeling, and whether you are in "flow." After a week, look for patterns. When are you most engaged? What activities produce the most flow? Use this data to restructure your life and work around your flow-producing activities.

### Strategy 2: Ask the Big Question {.practice-exercise}

At the end of each day, ask yourself: "Was I better today than yesterday?" This question -- focused on incremental improvement rather than dramatic transformation -- keeps you oriented toward mastery. You do not need to improve by leaps. You need to improve by inches, consistently, over time.

### Strategy 3: Take a Sagmeister {.practice-exercise}

Designer Stefan Sagmeister closes his studio for one year out of every seven to pursue personal projects, travel, and experimentation. While most people cannot take a full sabbatical, the principle applies at smaller scales: take a "mini-Sagmeister" -- a day, a week, or a month dedicated to exploration and renewal. Many breakthroughs happen during periods of deliberate rest and play.

### Strategy 4: Create Your Own FedEx Day {.practice-exercise}

Designate a 24-hour period to work on any project you want -- something that has nothing to do with your regular responsibilities. The only requirement: you must deliver something at the end. A prototype, a solution, a presentation. The constraint of the deadline combined with the freedom of the topic creates a powerful motivational cocktail.

### Strategy 5: Master Deliberate Practice {.practice-exercise}

Choose one skill you want to improve. Design a deliberate practice routine:

1. Set a stretch goal focused on a specific aspect of the skill
2. Practice with full concentration for a defined period (start with 20-30 minutes)
3. Seek immediate feedback (from a mentor, a recording, or measurable results)
4. Repeat, reflect, and adjust. Increase difficulty as you improve
5. Accept that deliberate practice is effortful and often uncomfortable -- that is the point

### Strategy 6: Get Unstuck with Oblique Strategies {.practice-exercise}

When you are stuck on a problem, try approaching it from a completely unexpected angle. Brian Eno and Peter Schmidt created a deck of "Oblique Strategies" cards with provocations like "What would your closest friend do?" or "Honor thy error as a hidden intention." The randomness breaks you out of fixed thinking patterns and can lead to creative breakthroughs.

### Strategy 7: Find Your Sentence {.practice-exercise}

Inspired by Clare Boothe Luce's advice to President Kennedy, distill your purpose into a single sentence. Abraham Lincoln: "He preserved the union and freed the slaves." Franklin Roosevelt: "He lifted us out of a great depression and helped us win a world war." What is your sentence? If you cannot articulate one, spend time exploring what drives you at the deepest level.

### Strategy 8: Perform a Performance Review -- on Yourself {.practice-exercise}

Once a month, sit down and give yourself a performance review. Set goals at the beginning of each month in three areas: learning goals, performance goals, and purpose goals. At the end of the month, assess your progress honestly. Where did you meet your goals? Where did you fall short? What adjustments will you make next month? This turns motivation from a vague aspiration into a concrete, measurable practice.

### Strategy 9: Create a Motivation Poster -- for Yourself {.practice-exercise}

Forget the generic motivational posters with eagles and sunsets. Create your own motivation poster that captures your personal "why." Include your one-sentence purpose, your key values, and the specific behaviors you want to cultivate. Put it somewhere you will see it every day. Personal, specific motivation beats generic inspiration every time.

## For Organizations: Nine Ways to Improve Your Company

### Organizational Strategy 1: Carve Out Time for Non-Commissioned Work {.type-i-box}

Implement some version of Google's 20% time or Atlassian's FedEx Days. Give employees structured time to work on projects of their choosing. The most innovative ideas in your organization are probably trapped inside people who do not have permission to pursue them. Give them that permission.

### Organizational Strategy 2: Conduct an Autonomy Audit {.type-i-box}

Survey your team on how much autonomy they have over the four T's: Task, Time, Technique, and Team. Compare their responses to management's perception. The gap between what leaders think they provide and what employees actually experience is often enormous -- and enormously instructive.

### Organizational Strategy 3: Take Three Steps Toward Giving Up Control {.type-i-box}

For leaders who find it difficult to relinquish control:

1. **Involve people in goal-setting** rather than dictating goals from above
2. **Use non-controlling language** -- say "think about" and "consider" rather than "must" and "should"
3. **Hold office hours** -- make yourself available for guidance, but let people come to you rather than hovering over them

### Organizational Strategy 4: Ensure Fair and Adequate Baseline Compensation {.motivation-box}

Pay people enough so that money is not an issue. This means paying at or above market rates and ensuring internal equity. Once baseline compensation is fair, additional monetary incentives have diminishing returns. But if baseline pay is unfair, no amount of autonomy or purpose will compensate.

Pink emphasizes that Type I motivation does not mean ignoring money. It means getting money off the table so that intrinsic motivation can flourish.

### Organizational Strategy 5: Get the Goals Right {.warning-box}

Be careful with goals. Research shows that overly specific, narrowly focused goals can produce unintended consequences: unethical behavior, excessive risk-taking, and diminished intrinsic motivation. When setting goals:

- Frame them as learning goals ("develop expertise in X") rather than pure performance goals ("hit target Y")
- Make them broad enough to allow for creative approaches
- Supplement quantitative targets with qualitative assessments
- Include "purpose goals" alongside financial goals

### Organizational Strategy 6: Reimagine the Way You Compensate {.type-i-box}

Consider moving beyond traditional commission and bonus structures. Alternatives include:

- **Pay-what-you-want models** for internal services
- **Team-based rewards** instead of individual competition
- **"Now that" bonuses** -- unexpected rewards given after exceptional work, rather than "if-then" incentives announced in advance
- **Non-monetary recognition** -- public acknowledgment, learning opportunities, choice assignments

### Organizational Strategy 7: Spend Time Talking About Purpose {.type-i-box}

Set aside time in team meetings to discuss the purpose behind the work. Ask: "Why does this project matter? Who benefits from what we do? How does our work contribute to the larger mission?" These conversations may feel awkward at first, but they remind people why their work matters -- and that reminder is a powerful motivator.

### Organizational Strategy 8: Use Peer-to-Peer Recognition {.type-i-box}

Create systems for employees to recognize and celebrate each other's contributions. Peer recognition is often more meaningful than top-down recognition because it comes from people who understand the work and the effort involved.

### Organizational Strategy 9: Start with Questions, Not Answers {.type-i-box}

When a challenge arises, resist the managerial instinct to provide the answer. Instead, ask questions: "What do you think we should do? What have you tried? What would you recommend?" This signals trust, develops problem-solving capabilities, and reinforces autonomy.

## For Parents and Educators

### Rethinking How We Motivate Children {.motivation-box}

Many of the motivational strategies used in schools and homes are straight out of the Motivation 2.0 playbook: grades, gold stars, allowance tied to chores, punishments for misbehavior. Pink argues that these approaches can undermine children's intrinsic motivation and love of learning just as effectively as they undermine adults' motivation at work.

### Three Principles for Parents and Educators {.type-i-box}

1. **Offer praise wisely.** Praise effort and strategy, not intelligence or talent. Carol Dweck's research shows that praising children for being "smart" creates a fixed mindset and makes them afraid of challenges. Praising them for working hard and using good strategies creates a growth mindset.

2. **Provide autonomy.** Give children meaningful choices whenever possible -- what to study, how to complete assignments, when to do homework. The more control children have over their learning, the more engaged they become.

3. **Help them find purpose.** Connect schoolwork to the real world. Help children see how what they are learning relates to problems they care about. When children understand why something matters, they are far more motivated to learn it.

### Turn Homework into Homelearning {.practice-exercise}

Rather than assigning rote homework, encourage children to pursue their own learning projects. Let them choose a topic that interests them and explore it in depth. Provide guidance and resources, but let the direction come from their own curiosity. The goal is to cultivate intrinsic motivation for learning, not merely compliance with assignments.

### The Allowance Question {.research-box}

Pink advises separating allowance from chores. When you pay children for household tasks, you transform a family obligation into a financial transaction -- and risk undermining their sense of responsibility. Instead, give allowance as a baseline (to teach financial literacy) and expect chores as a family contribution (to build responsibility and purpose).

### Reflection {.reflection}

Whether you are a leader, a parent, or an individual seeking greater motivation: which of these strategies resonates most with you? What is one thing you could implement this week -- not next month, not next year, but this week -- to move yourself or your organization from Type X toward Type I?

## The Big Picture

### From Motivation 2.0 to Motivation 3.0 {.principle-box}

The shift from Type X to Type I is not about rejecting all external rewards. It is about building a motivational system on a stronger foundation -- one rooted in our innate needs for autonomy, mastery, and purpose. External rewards still have a place, but they work best as a baseline, not as the primary driver.

## Key Takeaways

- Give yourself a flow test, ask "Was I better today than yesterday?", and distill your purpose into one sentence to activate Type I behavior.
- For organizations: conduct an autonomy audit, ensure fair baseline compensation, create space for non-commissioned work, and make purpose a regular conversation topic.
- Goals should be framed as learning goals, not just performance goals, and should include purpose alongside financial targets.
- For parents and educators: praise effort over talent, provide meaningful choices, separate allowance from chores, and help children connect learning to real-world purpose.
- The shift from Type X to Type I is not about eliminating rewards -- it is about building motivation on the stronger foundation of autonomy, mastery, and purpose.
- Start small: choose one strategy, implement it this week, and observe the results.

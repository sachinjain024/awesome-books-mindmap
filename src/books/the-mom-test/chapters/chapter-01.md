Every founder has had a version of this conversation: you tell your mom about your business idea, she says it sounds great, and you walk away feeling validated. But your mom is lying to you — not because she is malicious, but because she loves you. The Mom Test is a set of simple rules for crafting questions that even your mom cannot lie to you about.

## The Problem with Pitching Your Idea

Most founders approach customer conversations backward. They describe their idea, then ask if people think it is good. This is a terrible approach because almost everyone will tell you what you want to hear. People are polite. They do not want to crush your dreams over coffee.

> "The Mom Test is a set of simple rules for crafting good questions that even your mom can't lie to you about."
> — Rob Fitzpatrick

The problem is not that people are bad or dishonest. The problem is that you are asking questions that make it impossible for them to give you useful answers. When you pitch your idea and ask for opinions, you are begging for compliments, not collecting data.

### The Three Rules of The Mom Test {.principle-box}

1. **Talk about their life instead of your idea** — Ask about their problems, their workflows, their frustrations. Do not describe your solution.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future** — "When was the last time that happened?" beats "Would you ever use something like this?"
3. **Talk less and listen more** — You are there to learn, not to pitch. If you are talking more than they are, you are doing it wrong.

## Good Questions vs. Bad Questions

The difference between a useful conversation and a waste of time comes down to the questions you ask. Bad questions invite opinions and hypotheticals. Good questions uncover concrete facts about real behavior.

### Bad Questions to Avoid {.warning-box}

- "Do you think it's a good idea?" — Opinions are worthless. Everyone will say yes to be nice.
- "Would you buy a product that did X?" — Hypothetical future promises mean nothing.
- "How much would you pay for X?" — People are terrible at predicting their own spending.

### Good Questions to Ask {.practice-exercise}

- "What does your workflow look like for X?" — Reveals the real process and real pain points.
- "What is the hardest part about doing X?" — Uncovers where the real problems live.
- "When was the last time that happened?" — Anchors the conversation in specific, verifiable reality.
- "What have you already tried to solve this?" — Shows how much they actually care about fixing the problem.
- "How are you dealing with it now?" — Reveals existing solutions and switching costs.

## Example: A Cookbook App

Fitzpatrick uses a running example throughout the book — an entrepreneur thinking about building a cookbook app for the iPad. Here is how The Mom Test transforms the conversation.

### Bad Conversation {.warning-box}

**You:** "I'm thinking about building a cookbook app. Would you use it?"

**Mom:** "Of course, honey! That sounds like a great idea!"

This tells you nothing. She has not used cookbooks on her iPad. She is just being supportive.

### Good Conversation {.principle-box}

**You:** "How often do you cook from recipes?"

**Mom:** "Oh, I cook a few times a week, but I mostly just make the same six things."

**You:** "When was the last time you tried a new recipe?"

**Mom:** "Hmm, probably a few months ago for Thanksgiving. I found one online."

**You:** "What happened when you used that recipe?"

**Mom:** "It was fine. I had to keep scrolling on my phone and getting flour on the screen."

Now you are learning real facts: she rarely tries new recipes, she uses her phone not a tablet, and the experience has real friction points. None of this required pitching your idea.

## Talk Less, Listen More

> "You aren't allowed to tell them what their problem is, and in return, they aren't allowed to tell you what to build. They own the problem, you own the solution."
> — Rob Fitzpatrick

The most important skill in customer conversations is shutting up. Every moment you spend talking is a moment you are not learning. The goal of the conversation is to understand their world, not to explain yours. If you catch yourself talking for more than a couple of minutes straight, you are off track.

### Signs You Are Talking Too Much {.warning-box}

- You are explaining your idea in detail
- You are responding to every objection with "but what if we..."
- You feel the conversation is going well because they keep nodding
- You leave the meeting without learning anything surprising

## Key Takeaways

- The Mom Test has three rules: talk about their life, ask about specifics in the past, and listen more than you talk
- Bad questions fish for compliments and hypothetical promises
- Good questions uncover concrete behaviors and real pain points
- You own the solution; they own the problem — do not cross those boundaries
- If every conversation is validating your idea, you are probably asking the wrong questions
- Even your most supportive friends and family can give you useful data if you ask the right questions

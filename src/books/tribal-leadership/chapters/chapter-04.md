> "Stage 3 is the land of the 'lone warrior,' the person whose battle cry is 'I'm great.' And the unspoken, but felt, addition is 'and you're not.'"
> — Dave Logan, John King & Halee Fischer-Wright

Stage 3 is the most important stage to understand because it is where nearly half of all professional tribes operate. It is the culture of individual ambition, personal branding, and competitive superiority. People at Stage 3 are often the highest performers in an organization. They are smart, driven, and accomplished. But they are also the biggest obstacle to collective greatness because their personal success comes at the expense of tribal cohesion.

## The Stage 3 Worldview

At Stage 3, the individual has overcome the victimhood of Stage 2 and discovered personal competence. They know they are good at what they do, and they want everyone else to know it too. Their identity is built around personal achievement, expertise, and status.

### The Stage 3 Mindset {.stage-box}

- **"I know things you don't know"** — Knowledge is power, and sharing it freely would diminish their advantage
- **"I work harder than everyone else"** — Their success is earned and therefore deserved
- **"If you want something done right, do it yourself"** — Delegation is risky because others will not meet their standards
- **"I'll help you, but you owe me"** — Relationships are transactional
- **"Winning is what matters"** — And winning requires someone else to lose

Stage 3 is not inherently negative. It represents genuine growth from Stage 2. The person has developed real skills, earned genuine accomplishments, and built confidence. The problem arises when Stage 3 becomes the ceiling — when personal greatness is the only kind of greatness the person can imagine.

## The Language of Stage 3

Stage 3 language is easy to recognize once you know what to listen for. It is dominated by the first-person singular pronoun and by comparisons that elevate the speaker.

### Stage 3 Language Patterns {.language-box}

- **Self-promotion:** "Let me tell you about the deal I just closed." "I was the one who solved that problem."
- **Subtle put-downs:** "That's a good try." "You'll get there eventually." "Here's what you should have done."
- **Knowledge hoarding:** "I have a relationship with that client." (Implying: and you don't) "I'm the only one who really understands this system."
- **Competitive framing:** "I always exceed my targets." "My team outperformed everyone else."
- **Conditional help:** "Sure, I can help with that." (Subtext: and now you owe me)
- **Exhaustion as a badge:** "I was here until midnight." "I haven't taken a vacation in two years."

The unspoken subtext of all Stage 3 communication is "and you're not." It is rarely stated explicitly, but it is always felt. When a Stage 3 person says "I crushed my numbers this quarter," the implicit comparison is to everyone who did not.

## The Dyadic Relationship Structure

The defining structural feature of Stage 3 is the dyadic relationship. Stage 3 people build networks of two-person relationships with themselves at the center. They connect to each person individually but do not connect those people to each other.

### How Dyads Work at Stage 3 {.tribe-box}

Imagine a Stage 3 manager named Sarah. Sarah has separate relationships with each of her direct reports: Alex, Brian, and Carla. Sarah knows what each of them is working on, what their concerns are, and what information they need. But Alex, Brian, and Carla do not share this information with each other — they share it with Sarah.

This structure gives Sarah enormous power. She is the information hub, the relationship broker, the indispensable center of the network. If Alex needs something from Brian, Alex goes through Sarah. If Carla has a great idea, she brings it to Sarah, not to the team.

**The dyadic structure creates several predictable problems:**

- **Bottleneck:** Sarah becomes the limiting factor for every decision and every piece of information
- **Fragility:** If Sarah is unavailable, the team stalls
- **Politics:** Alex, Brian, and Carla compete for Sarah's attention and favor
- **Information asymmetry:** Sarah can selectively share or withhold information to maintain control
- **Burnout:** Sarah works 80-hour weeks because everything flows through her

### Why Stage 3 People Prefer Dyads {.principle-box}

Stage 3 people do not build dyadic structures maliciously. They do it because dyads reinforce their identity as the knowledgeable, indispensable expert. If Sarah introduced Alex directly to Brian and let them work together without her, she would lose a measure of control and status. At Stage 3, that feels threatening.

This is why Stage 3 people often say things like "I don't have time to explain it — I'll just do it myself." They are not merely impatient; they are protecting their position as the person who knows, the person who can, the person who is essential.

## Stage 3 in Professional Culture

Stage 3 is the dominant culture in most professional environments because the systems around professional life reward individual achievement. Consider how most professionals are developed and evaluated:

### The Systems That Reinforce Stage 3 {.stage-box}

- **Education:** Individual grades, individual diplomas, individual honors
- **Hiring:** Individual resumes, individual interviews, individual assessments
- **Performance reviews:** Individual targets, individual ratings, individual bonuses
- **Promotion:** Individual advancement, individual title, individual office
- **Professional identity:** "I am a doctor." "I am a partner." "I am a VP."

Every one of these systems teaches the lesson that personal excellence is the path to success. Is it any wonder that 49% of workplace tribes end up at Stage 3?

## The Limits of Stage 3

Despite its prevalence and its genuine strengths, Stage 3 culture has a hard ceiling. A tribe of Stage 3 individuals can produce impressive individual results, but it cannot produce the kind of collective achievement that defines truly great organizations.

### What Stage 3 Cannot Do {.principle-box}

- **Innovate collectively:** Breakthrough innovation requires the collision of diverse perspectives in an environment of trust, which dyadic structures prevent
- **Retain top talent long-term:** Stage 3 environments eventually burn out their best people
- **Build institutional knowledge:** When knowledge is hoarded by individuals, it leaves when they leave
- **Respond to complex challenges:** Problems that require cross-functional collaboration stall in dyadic networks
- **Create customer loyalty:** Customers sense when an organization is a collection of competing individuals rather than a unified tribe

The authors describe Stage 3 as a "glass ceiling" on organizational performance. No amount of individual talent can compensate for the lack of tribal cohesion. A Stage 3 organization full of brilliant people will consistently be outperformed by a Stage 4 organization with merely competent people.

## Moving from Stage 3 to Stage 4

The Stage 3 to Stage 4 transition is the most important and most difficult transition in the Tribal Leadership framework. It is the transition from "I'm great" to "We're great," from personal ambition to shared purpose, from dyads to triads.

### Why This Transition Is So Hard {.leverage-point}

Stage 3 people resist the transition to Stage 4 because it feels like giving up the very thing that has made them successful: their individual identity as someone who is great. The Stage 3 person has built their career, their reputation, and their self-concept around being the smartest person in the room. Moving to Stage 4 means admitting that the room might be smarter than any one person in it.

### Leverage Points for Stage 3 to 4 {.leverage-point}

- **Encourage triadic relationships:** When a Stage 3 person comes to you with a problem, introduce them to someone else who can help — and then step back. "You should talk to Jordan about that — you two would work well together."
- **Work on values:** Ask the Stage 3 person what they stand for beyond winning. What matters to them at a deeper level? Help them articulate values that transcend personal ambition.
- **Point to projects that require collaboration:** Give them challenges that cannot be solved alone, where they need others as partners, not subordinates.
- **Highlight the limits of their approach:** When they burn out, when they hit the ceiling, when they feel lonely at the top — these are moments of openness to Stage 4.
- **Name the noble cause:** Help them see that their work could serve a purpose larger than personal achievement.
- **Model Stage 4 yourself:** Be the leader who gives credit to the team, who builds triadic connections, who talks about "we" instead of "I."

### Practice: The Triadic Introduction {.practice-exercise}

This week, practice making triadic introductions. When someone comes to you with a need, instead of solving it yourself (which reinforces your Stage 3 hub position), connect them directly with someone who can help:

1. Identify two people who would benefit from knowing each other
2. Introduce them and explain why each would value the connection
3. Step back and let them develop their own relationship
4. Resist the urge to stay in the middle as the intermediary
5. Notice how this feels — if it feels like losing control, you have found your Stage 3 edge

### Reflection {.reflection}

Consider your own professional identity. How much of it is built around being the person who knows, the person who can, the person who is indispensable? What would it feel like to shift from "I'm great" to "We're great"? What are you afraid of losing in that transition? The answers to these questions reveal the emotional barrier between Stage 3 and Stage 4.

## Key Takeaways

- Stage 3 ("I'm great and you're not") is the dominant culture in 49% of workplace tribes and is characterized by individual competitiveness and knowledge hoarding
- Stage 3 operates through dyadic (two-person) relationships where the individual sits at the hub, controlling information flow
- Professional systems (education, hiring, performance reviews) systematically reinforce Stage 3 culture
- Stage 3 creates a hard ceiling on collective performance because it prevents the trust and collaboration needed for breakthrough results
- The Stage 3 to Stage 4 transition is the most difficult because it requires surrendering individual identity as "the great one"
- Key leverage points include building triadic relationships, articulating shared values, and assigning challenges that require genuine collaboration

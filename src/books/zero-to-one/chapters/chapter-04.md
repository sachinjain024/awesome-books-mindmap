This chapter digs deeper into why we are so drawn to competition and why that instinct is often destructive. Thiel argues that our educational system and culture train us to compete, and this training blinds us to the more valuable path of creating monopolies.

## Competition as Ideology

From childhood, we are taught that competition is healthy and natural. Schools rank students against each other. Businesses are told to compete aggressively. But Thiel argues that this ideology of competition is more like a destructive war than a healthy exercise.

> "Competition is an ideology — the ideology — that pervades our society and distorts our thinking. We preach competition, internalize its necessity, and enact its commandments; and as a result, we trap ourselves within it."
> — Peter Thiel

The education system is where competition ideology begins. Students compete for grades, for college admissions, for prestigious jobs. This competition escalates as people advance, consuming their energy and narrowing their thinking.

## War and Peace in Business

Thiel draws a direct analogy between business competition and actual war. Like real wars, business wars are costly, destructive, and often pointless. People lose sight of what matters and focus entirely on defeating the enemy.

### The Microsoft vs. Google Wars {.case-study}

Thiel illustrates this with the Microsoft-Google rivalry of the 2000s:

- Google launched Chrome to compete with Internet Explorer
- Microsoft launched Bing to compete with Google Search
- Google created Google Docs to compete with Microsoft Office
- Microsoft responded with its own versions of every Google product

While these two giants were distracted fighting each other, Apple quietly surpassed them both in market value. The rivalry consumed resources and attention that could have been directed at genuinely new creation.

## The Rivalry Trap

Thiel shares personal stories about how rivalry distorts judgment. At Stanford Law School, students competed fiercely for clerkships and prestigious positions. The competition itself became the goal, disconnected from any deeper purpose.

### Why We Fall Into Rivalry {.insight-box}

- **Mimetic desire:** We want what others want, not what is actually valuable. We copy each other's ambitions.
- **Narcissism of small differences:** The most intense rivalries are between people or companies that are very similar. The closer you are, the fiercer the competition.
- **Identity distortion:** Rivalries make us define ourselves by our opponents rather than by our own goals.
- **Escalation:** Competitions tend to intensify over time as both sides invest more in winning, even when winning no longer makes strategic sense.

## How to Escape Competition

If competition is destructive, the question becomes: how do you escape it? Thiel offers practical guidance for entrepreneurs.

### Paths Out of Competition {.strategy-box}

- **Differentiate radically:** Do not make a slightly better product. Create a category of one.
- **Avoid ego-driven rivalry:** If you find yourself in an escalating rivalry, consider whether it is worth continuing.
- **Merge or retreat:** Sometimes the best strategic move is to merge with a rival (as Thiel did with PayPal and X.com) rather than fight to the death.
- **Find blue ocean:** Build something so different that there is no direct competitor to fight.

> "If you can recognize competition as a destructive force instead of a sign of value, you're already more sane than most."
> — Peter Thiel

## The PayPal and X.com Merger

Thiel shares the story of PayPal's rivalry with Elon Musk's X.com. The two companies were burning through cash competing for the same customers in online payments. Rather than fight to mutual destruction, they merged in 2000 — a decision that saved both companies and created enormous value.

## Key Takeaways

- Competition is an ideology that we absorb from childhood without questioning
- Business rivalry, like war, is costly and often pointless — it consumes resources that could go to creation
- Mimetic desire drives us to compete for things we do not actually want
- The fiercest rivalries are between the most similar competitors
- Sometimes the best move is to merge with or sidestep a rival rather than fight
- Escaping competition is the first step toward building a monopoly

> -- Jocko Willink

## Leadership Flows Both Directions

Most people think of leadership as a top-down activity: the leader issues orders, and subordinates execute. But effective leadership flows in both directions. Leaders must lead those above them in the chain of command just as effectively as they lead those below. This chapter explores both dimensions and shows why failure in either direction cripples an organization.

### Frustration on the Ground {.battle-story}

Leif Babin describes the frustration that SEAL operators in Ramadi sometimes felt with higher headquarters. From the ground level, decisions made by senior leadership sometimes seemed disconnected from reality. Rules of engagement felt too restrictive. Requests for additional assets were denied or delayed. Operational boundaries seemed arbitrary.

It would have been easy -- and natural -- for Leif to commiserate with his SEALs. "Yeah, headquarters doesn't understand what we're dealing with." But doing so would have undermined the chain of command and made his team less effective. Instead, Leif applied Extreme Ownership to the situation.

If headquarters wasn't providing the support his team needed, the first question was not "What's wrong with headquarters?" It was "What have I failed to communicate?" If rules of engagement seemed too restrictive, the question was not "Why are they tying our hands?" It was "Have I clearly explained the tactical situation on the ground?"

### Leading Up the Chain {.battle-story}

Leif describes a specific instance where his SEAL platoon needed approval for an operation that required moving beyond their assigned sector. The request was initially denied by higher headquarters. Rather than complaining, Leif prepared a detailed brief explaining the tactical rationale, the intelligence supporting the operation, the risk mitigation measures in place, and the strategic value of the action.

He presented this brief to his commanding officer with professionalism and clarity. He didn't argue or push back emotionally. He provided the information and context that the senior leader needed to make an informed decision. The operation was approved.

The lesson was clear: when your boss doesn't support your plan, the most likely reason is that you haven't effectively communicated the information they need.

## The Principle: Leading Up

Leading up the chain of command is one of the most challenging aspects of leadership because it requires a subordinate to influence a superior -- someone with more authority, more experience, and often more ego. It requires tact, professionalism, and a genuine commitment to the mission over personal pride.

### How to Lead Up Effectively {.principle-box}

- **Take ownership of the relationship.** If your boss doesn't understand the situation on the ground, it's because you haven't communicated it effectively. Don't wait for them to ask -- proactively provide the information they need
- **Provide solutions, not just problems.** Every time you bring a problem to your boss, bring at least one recommended solution. This demonstrates initiative and makes their job easier
- **Use the boss's language.** Senior leaders think in terms of strategy, risk, and resources. Frame your requests in those terms, not in tactical jargon they may not relate to
- **Build trust through reliability.** The more reliably you execute, the more trust you earn. Trust creates the credibility needed to influence decisions
- **Choose your battles.** Not every disagreement is worth fighting. Save your influence capital for the issues that truly matter to mission success
- **Never go around the chain.** Going over your boss's head destroys trust and credibility. Work within the chain of command, even when it's frustrating

## The Principle: Leading Down

Leading down the chain of command is equally critical and equally challenging. The leader must communicate the big picture to subordinates while providing enough detail for effective execution.

### How to Lead Down Effectively {.principle-box}

- **Communicate the "why."** People execute better when they understand the purpose behind their tasks. Don't just issue orders -- explain the strategic context
- **Keep communication flowing.** Information gaps create anxiety, rumors, and disengagement. Even when you can't share everything, share what you can
- **Be accessible.** Junior team members must feel comfortable raising concerns, asking questions, and providing feedback. A leader who is unapproachable creates an information vacuum
- **Push information down before it's requested.** Don't wait for your team to ask why a decision was made. Proactively explain changes in direction, new priorities, and the reasoning behind them
- **Translate strategic language.** What makes sense at the executive level may not resonate on the front line. Translate corporate strategy into terms that are meaningful to the people who execute

### The Communication Gap {.warning-box}

The most common leadership failure in large organizations is the communication gap between levels of the chain of command. Senior leaders make decisions without explaining them. Middle managers pass along directives without context. Frontline employees execute tasks without understanding why. Each level blames the others for the disconnect.

The solution is for leaders at every level to take ownership of communication -- both up and down. If your boss doesn't understand the situation, it's your job to explain it. If your team doesn't understand the strategy, it's your job to translate it.

## Business Application: The Disconnected VP

### The Bridge Between Levels {.business-application}

Jocko describes a vice president who was struggling because she felt caught between her CEO and her direct reports. The CEO was pushing for rapid expansion into new markets. Her team was overwhelmed with current operations and resisting the additional workload. The VP felt stuck in the middle, unable to satisfy either side.

When coached to apply the principle of leading up and down, the VP realized she had been failing in both directions. She hadn't effectively communicated her team's capacity constraints to the CEO, leading to unrealistic expectations. And she hadn't effectively communicated the strategic rationale for expansion to her team, leading to resistance and resentment.

She took two specific actions. First, she prepared a clear, data-driven brief for the CEO showing current workload, capacity constraints, and a proposed timeline for expansion that was aggressive but realistic. The CEO, armed with information he hadn't previously had, adjusted his expectations.

Second, she sat down with her team and explained why the company was expanding: market opportunity, competitive pressure, and the growth potential that would create career opportunities for everyone on the team. Once the team understood the "why" and saw that the timeline was realistic, their resistance transformed into engagement.

### Strengthening Your Chain of Command {.practice-exercise}

- **Audit your upward communication.** Ask yourself: Does my boss have all the information they need to support my team? If not, what am I failing to communicate?
- **Audit your downward communication.** Ask your team: Do you understand why we're doing what we're doing? Listen to their answers honestly
- **Schedule regular updates.** Don't wait for crises to communicate. Regular, proactive updates build trust and prevent information gaps
- **Create feedback loops.** Establish mechanisms for information to flow both up and down. Town halls, skip-level meetings, anonymous surveys, and open-door policies all help
- **Take responsibility for misunderstandings.** When communication breaks down in either direction, assume it's your failure first. What could you have done differently to prevent the misunderstanding?

## The Leader as Translator

### Bridging the Gap {.discipline-box}

The most effective leaders in any organization serve as translators between levels. They take strategic vision from above and translate it into actionable tasks for their team. They take ground-level reality from below and translate it into strategic intelligence for their superiors. This translation function is perhaps the most valuable and underappreciated skill in leadership. Without it, organizations fracture along the seams of the chain of command, with each level operating in its own reality.

## Key Takeaways

- Leadership is not just top-down -- effective leaders lead both up and down the chain of command
- If your boss isn't supporting you, the first question is: "What have I failed to communicate?"
- Provide solutions, not just problems, when communicating up the chain
- Communicate the "why" to subordinates so they understand the purpose behind their tasks
- The leader serves as a translator between strategic vision and tactical reality
- Take ownership of communication failures in both directions -- never blame the chain of command

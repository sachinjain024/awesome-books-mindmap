Rune & the Cat

A Man Called Ove

Rune was Ove's neighbor and once his friend—until they fell out over cars (Saab vs. Volvo) and principles. Now Rune is ill and in a wheelchair; his wife Anita needs help. Ove doesn't want to get involved—but he does. A stray cat that Sonja would have loved adopts Ove. He complains about the cat—and feeds it. Both the past (Rune) and the present (the cat, the neighbors) are pulling Ove back into life.

Rune

Rune and Ove used to have coffee and fix things together. Then they disagreed—and stopped speaking. Now Rune can't speak at all. Ove helps Anita with the bureaucracy that wants to take Rune away to a home. In helping his old enemy, Ove is also making peace with the past. Forgiveness and duty go hand in hand.

"We always think there's enough time to do things with other people. Time to say things to them. Then something happens and then we've run out of time."
— Fredrik Backman, A Man Called Ove

The Cat

The cat is half-starved and stubborn. Ove doesn't want a cat—but Sonja would have taken it in. So Ove feeds it, lets it in, and complains the whole time. The cat becomes part of his routine and part of his heart. Small commitments—to a cat, to a neighbor—add up to a reason to stay.

Key Insight

Purpose doesn't have to be grand. It can be a cat, a neighbor's wife, a driving lesson. Ove finds his way back by doing the next small thing—and the next.

Key Takeaways

  • Old conflicts can be softened when we help each other in crisis.
  • There isn't always enough time—so we must act now.
  • Small commitments (a cat, a neighbor) can restore meaning.

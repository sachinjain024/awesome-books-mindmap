Meetings‚ÄîThe Medium of Managerial Work

Part Two: Management Is a Team Game

Introduction {.intro}

Most people complain about meetings. Grove takes a contrarian view: meetings are not interruptions to your work‚Äîthey ARE your work. The key is not fewer meetings, but better meetings. This chapter provides a framework for making meetings productive.

Meetings Are Necessary

A manager's work happens through meetings. Information is exchanged, decisions are made, relationships are built. Saying "meetings are a waste of time" is like a surgeon saying "operations are a waste of time."

"Before you are off and running to reorganize your life to reduce meetings, you should think about whether they are, in fact, a waste of time. My experience says they are not." ‚Äî Andy Grove

Two Types of Meetings

Grove classifies all meetings into two fundamental categories:

Meeting Types {.comparison-box}

Process-Oriented: Regularly scheduled, predictable format

  • One-on-ones
  • Staff meetings
  • Operation reviews

Mission-Oriented: Called for specific purpose, ends when resolved

  • Decision meetings
  • Problem-solving sessions
  • Ad-hoc discussions

The One-on-One Meeting

Grove considers the one-on-one the most important meeting type. It's the primary vehicle for information exchange between manager and subordinate.

One-on-One Essentials {.principle-box}

  • Frequency: Weekly or bi-weekly, depending on task-relevant maturity
  • Duration: At least one hour‚Äîenough time for depth
  • Owner: The subordinate owns the agenda and leads
  • Location: Subordinate's office or neutral territory, not manager's
  • Purpose: Mutual exchange of information and shared learning

Why the Subordinate Leads {.concept-box}

The one-on-one is the subordinate's meeting. They set the agenda because they know what they need help with. The manager listens, asks questions, coaches. This is far more productive than the manager interrogating.

"The one-on-one should be regarded as the subordinate's meeting, with its agenda and tone set by him... The supervisor is there to learn and to coach." ‚Äî Andy Grove

Staff Meetings

The staff meeting is where the manager and all direct reports come together. Its purpose is different from one-on-ones.

Staff Meeting Design {.principle-box}

  • Purpose: Peer interaction and group problem-solving
  • Role of manager: Facilitator, not lecturer
  • Ideal outcome: Subordinates learn from each other
  • Avoid: Status updates that could be done async

The manager should speak less than 20% of the time. The value comes from subordinates interacting with each other, not from hearing the manager talk.

Operation Reviews

These are larger meetings where knowledge is shared across organizational boundaries. Think of them as teaching sessions where one group presents to others.

Operation Review at Intel {.example-box}

One division presents its work to others. The audience learns; the presenters get feedback. This cross-pollination spreads knowledge that would otherwise stay siloed.

Mission-Oriented Meetings

These meetings are called to solve a specific problem or make a specific decision. They should have a clear purpose and end when that purpose is achieved.

Common Failure Mode {.warning-box}

Mission-oriented meetings often fail because:

  • The problem isn't clearly defined
  • The right people aren't in the room
  • There's no clear decision-maker identified
  • The meeting continues after the purpose is achieved

Mission Meeting Rules {.principle-box}

  1. Define the purpose clearly before starting
  2. Invite only those essential to the purpose
  3. Identify who will make the decision
  4. End the meeting when the purpose is achieved

Meeting Discipline

Bad meetings waste enormous amounts of organizational time. Grove insists on discipline:

Meeting Discipline Checklist {.leverage-box}

  • Start on time (don't punish the punctual)
  • Have an agenda (even if just for one-on-ones)
  • End with action items and owners
  • Cancel meetings that no longer serve their purpose
  • Question whether each meeting is still needed

Key Takeaways

  • Meetings are the medium of managerial work‚Äîthey ARE your job
  • Two types: process-oriented (regular) and mission-oriented (ad-hoc)
  • One-on-ones are the most important meeting; the subordinate should lead
  • Staff meetings are for peer interaction, not manager monologues
  • Mission-oriented meetings need clear purpose, right people, and known decision-maker
  • Meeting discipline (agendas, timing, action items) determines productivity

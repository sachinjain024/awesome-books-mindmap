About This Mind Map

This mind map presents the core teachings of The One Minute Manager, one of the most widely read management books ever published. Written as a parable, the book follows a young man searching for an effective manager and discovering three remarkably simple secrets that transform how leaders manage people and produce results.

Ken Blanchard and Spencer Johnson distilled decades of behavioral science research into a story that can be read in about an hour but applied for a lifetime. The three secrets — One Minute Goals, One Minute Praisings, and One Minute Redirects — form a complete management system built on clarity, positive reinforcement, and honest feedback delivered with respect.

Whether you are a new manager learning the fundamentals, a seasoned leader looking to simplify your approach, or someone who wants to understand how the best managers bring out the best in people, this mind map captures each secret in detail with practical steps you can begin using immediately.

Core Stoic Principles

  • One Minute Goals: Agree on goals together, write each on a single page, read and re-read each goal in about one minute, and regularly check whether behavior matches the goal.
  • One Minute Praisings: Catch people doing something right, praise them immediately and specifically, tell them how their good work makes you feel, and pause so they can feel the impact.
  • One Minute Redirects: Redirect immediately when something goes wrong, be specific about the mistake, share how you feel about it, then reaffirm that you value the person and believe in their ability.
  • People Produce Results: The best minute you spend is the one you invest in people. People who feel good about themselves produce good results, and people who produce good results feel good about themselves.
  • Simplicity is Power: Effective management does not require complex systems. The most powerful techniques are simple enough to use every day and take only a minute to apply.
  • Help People Reach Their Full Potential: The goal of management is not control but helping people manage themselves. Great managers develop people who no longer need to be managed.

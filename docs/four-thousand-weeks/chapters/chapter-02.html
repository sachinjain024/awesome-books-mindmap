Chapter 2: The Efficiency Trap

Why Productivity Makes You Busier

"The day will never arrive when you finally have everything under control, when the last email has been dealt with, when you can finally relax and turn your attention to the things that truly matter." — Oliver Burkeman

The Promise of Productivity

We've all been there: you discover a new productivity system, a better app, a more efficient workflow. You implement it with enthusiasm, and for a while, it works. You're getting through your tasks faster, your inbox is under control, you're checking items off your to-do list with satisfying efficiency.

But then something strange happens. Instead of feeling less busy, you feel more overwhelmed. Instead of having more free time, you have less. Your to-do list, despite your best efforts, never seems to shrink. In fact, it keeps growing. What's going on?

Welcome to the efficiency trap—one of the most counterintuitive and important concepts in this book.

The Mechanics of the Trap {.trap-box}

The efficiency trap works like this: the better you get at processing tasks, the more tasks you'll be given. The faster you answer emails, the more emails you'll receive. The more efficiently you complete projects, the more projects will land on your desk.

This isn't just about work. If you become known as someone who always has time to help friends, more friends will ask for your time. If you're efficient at household chores, you'll notice more chores that need doing. If you're good at organizing community events, you'll be asked to organize more of them.

The fundamental problem is that efficiency increases your capacity, and in our culture, increased capacity will always be filled with more demands.

Why the Trap is Inescapable

In previous eras, there were natural limits to how much could be asked of anyone. Before email, communication was slower and more deliberate. Before global markets, work had clearer boundaries. Before the internet, information and opportunities were more limited.

Today, those natural limits are gone. Email is instant and infinite. Work can follow you anywhere via smartphone. The stream of information, opportunities, and demands is effectively endless. No matter how efficient you become, there will always be more emails to answer, more content to consume, more experiences to have, more optimization to attempt.

This creates what Burkeman calls "existential overwhelm"—the feeling that the demands on your time exceed what any human could possibly fulfill, no matter how productive they become.

The Email Example {.insight-box}

Email perfectly illustrates the efficiency trap. When email was new, we treated it like physical mail—something you checked occasionally and responded to thoughtfully. But as we got better at processing email, as tools and techniques for "inbox zero" proliferated, something perverse happened: email expanded to fill the space we created.

Getting better at email doesn't free you from email; it just generates more email. Respond faster, and people come to expect faster responses. Clear your inbox efficiently, and more messages arrive to fill it. The people you correspond with adapt to your increased capacity by sending you more messages.

The same pattern applies to almost any area where you try to become more efficient. Efficiency doesn't free you from demands—it increases your capacity to meet demands, which then attracts more demands to fill that capacity.

The Cultural Context

Why is this trap so universal? Burkeman traces it to a fundamental shift in how modern culture views time and productivity.

Before the Industrial Revolution, time was largely experienced as cyclical—connected to seasons, daylight, natural rhythms. You worked at the pace the work required, and there were natural limits. You couldn't harvest faster than the crops were ready. You couldn't weave cloth faster than your hands could move.

The Industrial Revolution changed everything. Time became commodified—something to be measured, managed, and maximized. Factory owners discovered they could increase profits by making workers more efficient. This mindset spread beyond factories into every aspect of life.

Today, we've internalized this industrial mindset so thoroughly that we apply it to ourselves. We see our own time as a resource to be optimized, our own productivity as something to continually improve. We judge ourselves by our output and efficiency.

The Existential Dimension {.philosophy-box}

The efficiency trap has an existential dimension too. In a secular age where we don't believe in an afterlife, this life is all we have. That creates enormous pressure to make the most of our four thousand weeks—to experience as much as possible, achieve as much as possible, become as much as possible.

But "as much as possible" is infinite. The world offers an effectively unlimited number of experiences, achievements, and ways to grow. So we try to cram more in by becoming more efficient—traveling faster, learning quicker, multitasking through experiences.

This backfires. Rushing through experiences to fit more in actually diminishes the experiences themselves. Reading faster means retaining less. Traveling more efficiently means experiencing each place less deeply. Multitasking means engaging with nothing fully.

The efficiency trap at this level means that trying to experience more actually leaves us with less meaningful experience.

The Alternative: Accepting Inefficiency

So what's the solution? Burkeman argues it's not about finding a better productivity system or becoming even more efficient. The solution is to step off the efficiency treadmill entirely—to accept that you cannot and will not get on top of everything, and that this is perfectly okay.

This means making conscious choices about where to direct your finite attention and energy, knowing that many worthy things will go undone. It means accepting that your inbox will never be empty, your to-do list will never be completed, and there will always be more you could be doing.

It means asking not "How can I fit everything in?" but "What do I actually want to do with the small amount of time I have?"

Strategic Inefficiency {.solution-box}

One practical approach is what we might call strategic inefficiency—deliberately choosing to be less efficient in areas that don't truly matter to you, to protect your time and attention for what does matter.

This might mean:

  • Being slower to respond to non-essential emails, training people not to expect instant responses
  • Saying no to opportunities that don't align with your priorities, even good opportunities
  • Resisting the urge to optimize every moment, accepting that some "wasted" time is essential for being human
  • Setting boundaries on your availability, even if it means disappointing some people

The goal isn't to be lazy or irresponsible. It's to recognize that perfect efficiency isn't possible or even desirable, and that trying to achieve it actually makes your life worse.

Embracing the Constraint

The efficiency trap reveals a deeper truth: our limitations aren't obstacles to overcome but realities to embrace. You have finite time and finite energy. No system, no matter how sophisticated, can change that fundamental fact.

Once you accept this, the question shifts from "How can I do everything?" to "What deserves my limited time and attention?" This is a harder question in some ways, because it requires making difficult choices and accepting trade-offs. But it's also more honest and ultimately more liberating.

Key Takeaways

  • The efficiency trap: becoming more productive doesn't free up time; it creates more demands that fill the space you've created.

  • Email is the perfect example: the faster you process email, the more email you receive, creating an endless cycle of communication.

  • Increased capacity attracts increased demands: in our culture, any freed-up time or energy will be quickly filled with new tasks and obligations.

  • The cultural roots: our efficiency obsession stems from the Industrial Revolution's commodification of time and the modern secular pressure to maximize this one life.

  • Efficiency can diminish experience: rushing through more experiences to fit them all in actually makes each experience less meaningful and memorable.

  • The solution is strategic inefficiency: consciously choosing what not to optimize, accepting that many things will go undone, and focusing your limited resources on what truly matters.

How Do You Change the System?

Systemic change vs. point solutions

‚ÄúAt some point, you have to stop pulling people out of the river and go upstream to change the system that‚Äôs pushing them in.‚Äù ‚Äî Dan Heath

Point Solutions vs. Systemic Change

Every upstream intervention operates at some level of the causal chain. Some interventions address immediate proximate causes ‚Äî the specific trigger for a specific problem in a specific case. Others go deeper, addressing the structural conditions that generate problems across many cases.

Heath calls the former ‚Äúpoint solutions‚Äù and the latter ‚Äúsystemic change.‚Äù Both are valuable, but they operate differently, require different skills, and achieve different scales of impact.

A point solution might be: providing housing assistance to a family about to be evicted, preventing the housing instability that would lead to school absenteeism. This helps this family, at this moment.

A systemic change might be: reforming housing assistance programs so that fewer families face eviction in the first place, or changing zoning laws so that more affordable housing exists in areas near good schools. This helps thousands of families who are not yet in crisis.

How Deep Should You Go?

There is no universal answer to how far upstream you should go. Going further upstream generally means:

  • Larger potential impact ‚Äî systemic changes prevent more problems across more people
  • Longer time horizon ‚Äî systemic change takes years to implement and years more to show results
  • More political complexity ‚Äî systems are maintained by stakeholders who benefit from them
  • More uncertainty ‚Äî the causal chains are longer and more complex

Going too far upstream without the resources, relationships, and time horizon to achieve change can be as fruitless as staying entirely downstream. Effective upstream thinking often means going to the farthest upstream point you can realistically influence ‚Äî and being honest about where that is.

The ACEs Research: Going to the Root

One of the most important upstream research programs in history is the Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) study, conducted by Kaiser Permanente and the Centers for Disease Control in the 1990s. The study found that adverse childhood experiences ‚Äî including abuse, neglect, and household dysfunction ‚Äî were significantly more common than expected in the general adult population, and that they had profound long-term effects on adult health.

ACEs and Adult Disease

Adults with 4+ ACEs were:

  • 2x more likely to develop heart disease
  • 4x more likely to develop chronic lung disease
  • 12x more likely to have attempted suicide
  • Significantly more likely to smoke, drink heavily, use drugs, and be obese

The implication is upstream and profound: a large proportion of adult chronic disease has its roots in childhood experiences. Preventing adverse childhood experiences ‚Äî through better parental support, healthcare access, poverty reduction, and domestic violence prevention ‚Äî could have enormous effects on adult health decades later.

This is upstream thinking at its most ambitious: preventing adult disease by improving childhood environments, through systemic changes in healthcare, social services, education, and housing.

The Bright Spots Strategy

When systemic change seems impossibly large, Heath suggests a practical starting point: find the bright spots ‚Äî places, programs, or communities where the problem is significantly smaller despite similar circumstances ‚Äî and figure out what‚Äôs different there.

Learning From Success

The bright spots approach asks: ‚ÄúWho has already solved this problem, at least partially, and what can we learn from them?‚Äù

If one school in a district has a 5% dropout rate while comparable schools have 35% rates, that school is a bright spot. The question is not how to impose some external solution ‚Äî it is how to understand and replicate whatever this school is doing differently.

This approach is powerful because it avoids the assumption that problems require brand-new solutions. Often, the solution already exists somewhere. The challenge is finding it, understanding it, and scaling it.

Becoming an Upstream Thinker

The final message of Upstream is personal: becoming an upstream thinker is a practice, not a credential. It is a way of looking at the world that asks, habitually, ‚ÄúWhy does this problem exist? What is causing it? Who else is involved? What would it take to prevent it?‚Äù

The Daily Practice of Upstream Thinking

Heath suggests several habits for cultivating upstream thinking:

  • Notice patterns, not just incidents: when you see the same type of problem recurring, resist the impulse to solve the specific case and instead ask what‚Äôs generating the pattern
  • Ask ‚Äúwho else is part of this?‚Äù ‚Äî most persistent problems involve multiple stakeholders; identify them and explore whether coordination could prevent what individual action cannot
  • Protect time for prevention ‚Äî explicitly schedule time for upstream thinking before the calendar fills with downstream crises
  • Celebrate invisible successes ‚Äî make explicit the things that didn‚Äôt go wrong because of prevention efforts, so those efforts remain visible and funded

Reflection

What is one problem in your life or work that you‚Äôve been addressing exclusively downstream? What would the first step of an upstream investigation look like ‚Äî tracing one level back toward the cause?

Key Takeaways

  • Point solutions address specific instances; systemic change addresses the structural conditions that generate instances ‚Äî both are valuable, but at very different scales
  • Going further upstream generally means greater potential impact but longer timelines, more political complexity, and more uncertainty about causal chains
  • The ACEs research shows that adult chronic disease is significantly rooted in adverse childhood experiences ‚Äî one of the most powerful demonstrations of the upstream/downstream connection in human health
  • The bright spots strategy ‚Äî finding where the problem is smaller in comparable contexts and understanding why ‚Äî is a powerful starting point for systemic change without requiring a blank-slate solution
  • Becoming an upstream thinker is a daily practice of noticing patterns, asking why, coordinating across silos, and protecting time for prevention
  • The measure of an upstream thinker is not just the problems they prevent ‚Äî it‚Äôs the habit of mind they bring to every problem they encounter

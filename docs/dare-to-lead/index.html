About This Book

Brene Brown spent two decades studying courage, vulnerability, shame, and empathy. In Dare to Lead, she distills her research into a practical guide for leaders who want to build brave cultures where people feel safe enough to innovate, take risks, and have the tough conversations that drive meaningful progress.

The book is organized around four skill sets that Brown’s research identifies as essential for courageous leadership: rumbling with vulnerability, living into our values, braving trust, and learning to rise. Each is learnable, measurable, and teachable — which means daring leadership is not about personality or title, but about practice.

This mind map breaks down each of the four parts into its essential concepts, frameworks, and practices. Whether you lead a team of two or an organization of thousands, these ideas offer concrete tools for showing up with courage, building trust, and creating cultures where people dare to lead.

Core Stoic Principles

  • Vulnerability Is Courage: Vulnerability is not weakness. It is the most accurate measure of courage. Brave leaders are those willing to show up when they cannot control the outcome.
  • Clear Is Kind, Unclear Is Unkind: Avoiding tough conversations to spare feelings is not kind — it is unkind. Clarity, even when uncomfortable, respects people enough to tell them the truth.
  • Armor Up vs. Dare to Lead: We all develop armor to protect ourselves from vulnerability. Daring leaders recognize their armor — perfectionism, cynicism, numbing — and choose courage instead.
  • The BRAVING Inventory: Trust is not built in grand gestures. It accumulates through small, everyday behaviors captured in the BRAVING acronym — Boundaries, Reliability, Accountability, Vault, Integrity, Non-judgment, Generosity.
  • Values as Behaviors: Values are meaningless unless translated into specific, teachable, measurable, and observable behaviors. Living into values means practicing them daily, especially under pressure.
  • The SFD (Stormy First Draft): When we fail or face setbacks, our brains immediately construct stories — often inaccurate. The "story I'm making up" practice surfaces these narratives so we can interrogate and rewrite them.
  • Rumble Language: Daring leaders use rumble language to engage in productive conflict — phrases like "the story I'm making up," "I'm curious about," "tell me more," and "walk me through" create psychological safety.

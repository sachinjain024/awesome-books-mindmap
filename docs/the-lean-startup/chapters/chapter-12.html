Chapter 12: Innovate

Part Three: Accelerate

How can established companies maintain a startup's innovative spirit? This chapter addresses the challenge of fostering innovation within larger organizations. The key is creating the right environment for internal startup teams while protecting both the parent organization and the new venture.

The Innovation Challenge

As companies grow, they develop processes optimized for their current business. These processes—which are necessary for efficiency—often kill innovation. The challenge is creating space for startup-style experimentation within an established organization.

The Innovator's Dilemma

Established companies are often disrupted not because they're incompetent, but because their processes are optimized for the wrong thing. What makes them successful at their current business makes them unable to pursue new opportunities.

How to Nurture Disruptive Innovation

Ries identifies three key attributes that internal startup teams need:

The Three Requirements for Internal Startups

  1. Scarce but secure resources: The team needs less money than a mature division, but the funding must be protected from being cut during budget cycles.
  2. Independent authority: The team needs autonomy to make decisions without going through normal approval processes.
  3. Personal stake in the outcome: Team members need incentives tied to the startup's success, not the parent company's.

Without all three, internal startups fail. Starve them of resources and they can't experiment. Subject them to normal bureaucracy and they can't move fast. Remove personal incentives and the best people won't join.

Creating an Innovation Sandbox

One practical approach is the innovation sandbox: a protected environment where startup teams can experiment without endangering the core business.

Rules for the Innovation Sandbox

  • Any team can create a true split-test experiment that affects only the sandboxed parts of the product or service
  • One team must see the whole experiment through from end to end
  • No experiment can run longer than a specified time period
  • No experiment can affect more than a specified number of customers
  • Every experiment must be evaluated using a single standard report of five to ten actionable metrics
  • Every team must use the same metrics to evaluate their experiments

The sandbox provides safety rails while enabling real experimentation with real customers. Teams can move fast without risk of breaking the core business.

Protecting the Parent Organization

Innovation teams need protection from the parent organization—but the parent also needs protection from the innovation team.

Key Insight

The parent organization fears that the startup team will embarrass them, break things, or waste resources. The startup team fears that the parent will shut them down, slow them down, or take credit for their success. Both fears are legitimate.

Case Study: Intuit's Innovation Process

Intuit created a structured innovation process that protects both sides:

  • Small teams with dedicated resources
  • Clear milestones based on validated learning, not business metrics
  • Executive sponsors who protect the team from corporate antibodies
  • Defined integration path if the innovation succeeds

The Role of the Executive Sponsor

Internal startups need an executive sponsor—someone with enough authority to protect the team from interference while also holding them accountable.

Executive Sponsor Responsibilities

  1. Protect: Shield the team from corporate bureaucracy and political attacks
  2. Provide: Ensure the team has the resources they need
  3. Hold accountable: Require the team to demonstrate validated learning
  4. Integrate: Help successful innovations transition to the core business
  5. Kill: Shut down experiments that aren't working (compassionately)

Becoming the Status Quo

The ultimate challenge: successful startups eventually become the establishment they once disrupted. How do you maintain a culture of innovation as you grow?

"The only way to win is to learn faster than anyone else."
— Eric Ries

The answer is to institutionalize the Lean Startup methodology itself—not as a one-time technique but as a permanent capability. Innovation accounting, validated learning, and rapid experimentation become part of how the organization operates, not just how it starts.

Key Takeaways

  • Established companies need special structures to foster innovation
  • Internal startups need: secure resources, independent authority, and personal stakes
  • The innovation sandbox lets teams experiment without risking the core business
  • Both the parent organization and innovation teams need protection from each other
  • Executive sponsors are crucial for protecting and holding teams accountable
  • Institutionalize innovation capabilities, don't just run one-time programs

Chapter 4: Experiment

Part One: Vision

This chapter establishes that every startup should be viewed as a grand experiment. Rather than asking "Can this product be built?" we should ask "Should this product be built?" and "Can we build a sustainable business around this product?" The scientific method provides the framework for finding answers.

From Business Plan to Experiment

Traditional business plans are treated as documents to execute. But in a startup, a business plan is really a set of hypotheses that need to be tested. Instead of writing a plan and then executing it, startups should design experiments to test whether the plan's assumptions are true.

The Experimental Mindset

Think of your startup as a series of experiments. Each feature you build, each marketing campaign you run, each new market you enter is an experiment designed to test a hypothesis about your business.

"The goal of every startup experiment is to discover how to build a sustainable business around that vision."
— Eric Ries

The Two Most Important Questions

Before investing significant resources, every startup should answer two fundamental questions:

The Fundamental Questions

  1. Do customers recognize that they have the problem you are trying to solve?
  2. If there was a solution, would they buy it?

These questions seem obvious, but many startups skip them. They assume they know the answers based on their own intuition or limited conversations. The experimental approach requires testing these assumptions with real customer behavior.

The Zappos Experiment

Ries shares the founding story of Zappos as a perfect example of experimentation before investment.

Case Study: Zappos Tests Demand

Zappos founder Nick Swinmurn wanted to test whether customers would buy shoes online. Instead of building a warehouse full of inventory, he went to local shoe stores, took pictures of their products, and posted them online. When customers ordered, he bought the shoes from the store and shipped them.

The experiment tested: Would customers buy shoes without trying them on? The answer was yes—validated by actual purchases, not surveys.

This "Wizard of Oz" approach let Zappos learn about customer demand with minimal investment. They discovered not just that people would buy shoes online, but what the actual customer experience challenges would be.

Beyond "Will It Work?"

Experiments should be designed to answer specific questions about your business model, not just whether your product "works." Consider these dimensions:

Value Hypothesis
Does the product deliver value to customers?
Growth Hypothesis
How will new customers discover the product?
Revenue Model
Will customers pay, and how much?
Cost Structure
Can you deliver the product profitably?

The Village Laundry Service

Another example demonstrates how experimentation works in non-tech contexts.

Case Study: Village Laundry Service (VLS)

VLS in India wanted to provide laundry service to people in villages who had no access to washing machines. Instead of building a full operation, they:

  • Set up a single kiosk with a rented washer and dryer
  • Tested different pricing and service options
  • Learned about customer needs and willingness to pay

This minimal experiment taught them about their actual customers before major investment.

Designing Good Experiments

Not all experiments are created equal. A good startup experiment has specific characteristics:

Elements of a Good Experiment

  1. Clear hypothesis: What specific assumption are you testing?
  2. Measurable outcome: How will you know if the hypothesis is true or false?
  3. Real customers: Test with actual potential customers, not friends or family
  4. Minimum investment: Use the least resources needed to get a clear answer
  5. Actionable results: The outcome should tell you what to do next

Key Insight

An experiment that can't fail isn't really an experiment. Design tests that could prove you wrong—that's the only way to validate that you're right.

Key Takeaways

  • Treat your startup as a series of experiments, not a plan to execute
  • Test whether customers have the problem and would pay for a solution before building
  • Design experiments that test specific hypotheses with measurable outcomes
  • Use minimal resources to get clear answers before major investment
  • Real customer behavior is the only valid test—surveys and opinions aren't enough

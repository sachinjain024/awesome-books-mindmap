The Consistency Myth

Leadership development has long emphasised consistency. Be consistent in your values. Be consistent in your expectations. Be consistent in how you treat people. This advice contains important truth ‚Äî and an important misunderstanding.

Consistency in values and character is essential. Your team must be able to trust that your word means what it says, that your values are non-negotiable, and that you will behave according to clear principles.

Consistency in style ‚Äî in how you manage, communicate, and engage with every individual in every situation ‚Äî is not just unhelpful. It actively undermines your effectiveness.

Steven Bartlett‚Äôs law: You must be an inconsistent leader. The best leaders adapt their style fluidly to the individual and the situation, rather than applying one approach regardless of context.

Situational Leadership

Hersey and Blanchard's situational leadership model was one of the first formal frameworks to recognise that effective leadership is not a fixed style but a dynamic response to the maturity and motivation of each team member. A new hire needs different guidance than a ten-year veteran. A confident team member needs different engagement than an anxious one. The leader who responds to all of these the same way is consistently suboptimal.

The Four Leadership Modes

Bartlett describes the effective leader as operating in multiple modes, deployed as the situation demands:

1. Empathetic and supportive: When a team member is struggling, overwhelmed, or going through difficulty, the leader needs to be emotionally present, understanding, and supportive. Demanding performance in this moment creates defensiveness and closes down.

2. Challenging and demanding: When a team member is capable but coasting, performing below their potential, or needs to be stretched, the leader needs to create productive discomfort. Empathy in this moment enables underperformance.

3. Coaching and questioning: When a team member has the capability to solve a problem but needs to develop their own thinking, the leader asks rather than tells. Providing the answer in this moment robs them of the growth.

4. Directing and deciding: When speed is critical, ambiguity is harmful, or the stakes are high enough to require the leader‚Äôs specific judgement, the leader takes charge and is clear. Coaching in this moment wastes time and creates confusion.

The Core Law

Adapt your leadership style to the individual and the situation. The mode that serves one person in one context actively undermines another person in another context. Rigidity of style is a failure to lead.

Reading the Individual

The starting point of situational leadership is genuine interest in and understanding of each individual team member. Different people have different:

  • Levels of experience and competence in specific tasks
  • Levels of confidence and emotional stability
  • Communication preferences
  • Motivation drivers
  • Responses to challenge vs. support

The leader who invests time in understanding each individual ‚Äî through curiosity, observation, and genuine relationship ‚Äî is equipped to deploy the right style in the right moment.

The Individual Map

For each person on your team, map:

  1. Their current capability level in their primary responsibilities (developing, capable, excellent)
  2. Their current motivation level (struggling, engaged, highly driven)
  3. Their preferred communication style (direct, collaborative, detailed, high-level)
  4. The leadership mode that would most serve them right now
  5. The mode you currently default to with them ‚Äî and whether it matches what they need

The Emotional Intelligence Prerequisite

Inconsistent leadership ‚Äî in Bartlett‚Äôs sense ‚Äî requires high emotional intelligence: the ability to read emotional states, understand motivations, manage your own reactions, and deploy different responses without triggering defensiveness or confusion.

This is the advanced skill that separates great leaders from technically competent ones. Anyone can master a single leadership style. The ability to read the room, the person, and the moment ‚Äî and respond in whatever way best serves them ‚Äî is what makes leadership transformational rather than merely managerial.

Key Takeaways

  • Consistency in values and character is essential; consistency in leadership style is a trap
  • Different individuals in different situations require completely different leadership approaches
  • The four leadership modes: empathetic/supportive, challenging/demanding, coaching/questioning, directing/deciding
  • Reading the individual ‚Äî their capability, motivation, and preferences ‚Äî is the foundation of effective situational leadership
  • High emotional intelligence is the prerequisite for inconsistent leadership ‚Äî it enables reading and responding to what each moment requires

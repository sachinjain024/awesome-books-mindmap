Leverage the Power of Progress

Law 31 of 33
Pillar IV: The Team

‚ÄúProgress is the most powerful motivator available to a leader. Make it visible.‚Äù ‚Äî Steven Bartlett

The Progress Principle

Most leaders assume that employee motivation is primarily driven by compensation, recognition, and career advancement. These matter. But research reveals that the single most powerful daily motivator for knowledge workers is something far simpler and more accessible: the sense of making meaningful progress in meaningful work.

Teresa Amabile‚Äôs decade-long research at Harvard Business School ‚Äî tracking the daily inner work life of hundreds of knowledge workers ‚Äî identified what she called the Progress Principle: small wins in meaningful work create positive emotions, higher intrinsic motivation, and better cognitive performance. On the best days ‚Äî the days when people feel most engaged and productive ‚Äî the most common experience was making progress on work that mattered.

The Inner Work Life System

Amabile's research identified that people's inner work life ‚Äî the ongoing stream of emotions, perceptions, and motivations about their work ‚Äî profoundly affects performance. And the most important influence on inner work life is not pay, not recognition, not relationships ‚Äî it is the perception of making progress. A day in which visible progress was made is rated as a good day, regardless of other factors.

Why Progress Motivates More Than Destinations

Human beings are progress-seeking creatures. We are wired for forward movement. The achievement of a goal is often momentarily satisfying, but the sustained motivation comes from the journey ‚Äî the sense of moving forward, growing, and getting closer.

This creates a powerful leadership implication: the leader‚Äôs job is not only to set ambitious goals, but to make progress toward them visible, frequent, and celebrated. A team that can see itself moving forward ‚Äî even incrementally ‚Äî maintains the energy for the long journey in ways that teams focused only on the distant destination cannot.

The Core Law

Make progress visible, frequent, and celebrated. Small wins matter enormously to sustained motivation. Design your team's experience so that forward movement is felt daily, not just at annual milestones.

How to Engineer Progress

Break goals into small milestones: The ambitious annual goal should be broken into quarterly, monthly, weekly, and daily milestones. Each milestone achieved is a progress moment ‚Äî an opportunity to generate the motivational fuel of forward movement.

Make progress visible: Progress that is not seen is not felt. Dashboards, scoreboards, progress bars, weekly team updates, and regular check-ins that celebrate movement create the shared experience of forward momentum.

Eliminate progress inhibitors: Bartlett identifies that leaders often inadvertently destroy progress experiences through unclear priorities, shifting targets, excessive bureaucracy, and political obstacles that prevent people from doing their best work.

Celebrate small wins publicly: Small wins celebrated publicly do two things simultaneously: they generate intrinsic motivation for the people who achieved them, and they signal to the entire team that progress at any scale is valued.

Progress Engineering

For your current team or project:

  1. What is the most important goal, and what is the smallest unit of daily progress toward it?
  2. How would you know if the team made meaningful progress today? Is that visible to everyone?
  3. What is the last small win you publicly celebrated with the team?
  4. What is the single biggest obstacle preventing your team from experiencing progress? How could you remove it?

The Opposite: Progress Inhibitors

Just as progress generates motivation, the experience of being blocked, set back, or prevented from doing meaningful work generates the most powerful negative emotions in work life. Bartlett calls these progress inhibitors ‚Äî and eliminating them may be as important as creating progress experiences.

Common progress inhibitors:

  • Unclear or shifting priorities
  • Inadequate resources or information
  • Unnecessary bureaucratic processes
  • Micromanagement that prevents autonomy
  • Political obstacles that block good work

Leaders who systematically remove these inhibitors give their teams the gift of unobstructed progress ‚Äî and unobstructed progress generates the motivation that sustains great work.

Key Takeaways

  • The Progress Principle: the single most powerful daily motivator for knowledge workers is the sense of making meaningful progress in meaningful work
  • Small wins in meaningful work generate positive emotions, intrinsic motivation, and better cognitive performance
  • Progress that is not visible is not felt ‚Äî make forward movement visible through dashboards, milestones, and public celebration
  • Eliminating progress inhibitors is as important as creating progress experiences
  • Leaders who break ambitious goals into frequent milestones give their teams the sustained motivational fuel for long journeys

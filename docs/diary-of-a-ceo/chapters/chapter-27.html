The Discipline Equation

Bartlett closes the Philosophy pillar with what he calls the Discipline Equation ‚Äî a framework for understanding what actually creates the sustained, consistent action that distinguishes exceptional achievers from ordinary ones.

The equation has three variables:

Success = (Goal Value √ó Process Engagement) √∑ Process Friction

  • Goal Value: How much you genuinely want the outcome ‚Äî your burning desire
  • Process Engagement: How much you enjoy or find meaning in the actual work required
  • Process Friction: How difficult, inconvenient, or aversive the process feels

Increase goal value or process engagement, or decrease process friction, and discipline follows naturally. Most productivity advice focuses on willpower ‚Äî a way of pushing through process friction. Bartlett argues this is the least efficient approach.

Memento Mori ‚Äî Remember You Will Die

The ancient Stoic practice of memento mori ‚Äî meditating on the inevitability of death ‚Äî is one of the most powerful tools for generating urgency and focus. When Steve Jobs was diagnosed with cancer and thought he might have a short time left, he famously described the clarity it brought: "Almost everything ‚Äî all external expectations, all pride, all fear of embarrassment or failure ‚Äî these things just fall away in the face of death, leaving only what is truly important." This is not morbid ‚Äî it is focusing.

Death as the Ultimate Urgency Engine

The central insight of this chapter is that awareness of mortality is the most powerful source of urgency available. When you genuinely internalise that your time is finite ‚Äî that there is a definite number of mornings remaining ‚Äî the trivial things that consume most of your time become obviously trivial, and the important things become obviously important.

Most people live as though they have unlimited time. This unconscious assumption is the enemy of discipline. Bartlett prescribes regularly confronting mortality ‚Äî not as an exercise in depression, but as a focusing mechanism that burns away the non-essential and reveals what genuinely matters.

The Core Law

Use the awareness of finite time to create genuine urgency. Increase goal value through connection to meaningful outcomes. Reduce process friction through environmental design and gamification. The result is discipline that does not depend on willpower.

The Three Levers of Discipline

1. Increase Goal Value through Mortality Awareness Connect your goals to meaning, legacy, and finite time. Ask: ‚ÄúIf I had five years to live, would I prioritise this?‚Äù The answer clarifies what is genuinely worth disciplined pursuit.

2. Increase Process Engagement through Gamification Make the process itself more engaging ‚Äî not just endurable. Track streaks, create challenges, introduce variety, build in milestones and celebrations. When the process has its own rewards, discipline costs less.

3. Reduce Process Friction through Environmental Design Remove obstacles between intention and action. Lay out your running shoes the night before. Keep healthy food at eye level and unhealthy food out of sight. Make the desired behaviour the path of least resistance.

The Discipline Audit

For one important discipline you struggle to maintain:

  1. Goal Value Test: Can you articulate why this matters in terms of your life's most important priorities? If not, consider whether the goal is genuinely important or just intellectually appealing.
  2. Process Engagement: What would make the process itself more enjoyable or meaningful?
  3. Friction Map: What is the single biggest source of friction between intention and action? How could you remove it?
  4. Mortality Prompt: Imagine this is the last year of your life. Does maintaining this discipline feel more or less important from that vantage point?

The 1984 Anti-Sikh Riots

Following Indira Gandhi‚Äôs assassination, organized mobs attacked Sikhs across North India. Delhi was worst affected. For three days, Sikhs were hunted, burned alive, and killed while police stood by. Over 3,000 died.

The Babri Masjid in Ayodhya, built in the 16th century, stood on a site Hindus claimed was Ram‚Äôs birthplace. Hindu activists demanded the mosque be demolished and a temple built. The issue had simmered for decades; now it exploded.

The rath yatra deliberately inflamed Hindu-Muslim tensions. Each riot produced a Hindu backlash that benefited the BJP. V.P. Singh‚Äôs government arrested Advani, but the damage was done. Hindu nationalism was now a mass movement.

The Demolition

On December 6, 1992, a mob of Hindu activists (kar sevaks) demolished the Babri Masjid while BJP leaders watched. The demolition violated court orders and government assurances. It triggered riots across India that killed over 2,000.

A Constitutional Failure

The BJP-led Uttar Pradesh government had promised to protect the mosque. The central government had intelligence about the demolition plan. Yet neither acted effectively. The state‚Äôs failure to prevent the demolition remains a constitutional scandal.

Following the demolition, Mumbai (then Bombay) exploded in communal violence‚Äîfirst Muslim attacks on Hindus, then organized Hindu retaliation. The police were complicit. The Shiv Sena‚Äôs role was documented but rarely prosecuted.

The Legacy

The Ayodhya movement transformed Indian politics. The BJP emerged as a major national party. Hindu-Muslim relations were damaged for a generation. The demolition remains a source of legal battles and political controversy.

Long-term Consequences

The Ram Janmabhoomi movement made Hindu nationalism electorally viable. It also deepened Muslim insecurity and alienation. The demolition violated the secular compact on which India was founded. Its wounds have not healed.

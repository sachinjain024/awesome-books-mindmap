Chapter 4: OKRs — Objectives and Key Results

Part Two: Execution

OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a framework for setting and tracking goals. An Objective is the qualitative goal; Key Results are the measurable outcomes that show you've achieved it. Used well, OKRs align the organization, create focus, and make progress visible without micromanagement.

What Are OKRs?

An Objective is a clear, inspiring goal (e.g., "Become the preferred choice for small businesses in our segment"). Key Results are 2–5 measurable outcomes that define success (e.g., "Achieve NPS of 50+," "Reach 10K active SMB customers"). Key Results should be measurable—usually a number—and verifiable. If you hit the Key Results, you've achieved the Objective.

OKR Example

Objective: Ship a reliable, loved product that users recommend.

Key Results:

  • Reduce P0 incidents to zero for 30 consecutive days
  • Achieve NPS of 40+ from enterprise segment
  • Ship 3 major features from the roadmap on time

Why Use OKRs

OKRs create alignment: everyone can see how their work connects to company goals. They force prioritization—you can't have 20 objectives. They make progress visible so you can course-correct. And they separate "what we're committed to" (OKRs) from "what we'd like to do" (backlog), reducing confusion about what matters most.

Key Insight

OKRs are not a performance evaluation tool. They're a planning and alignment tool. If you tie OKR achievement directly to bonus or rating, people will sandbag or game the numbers. Use OKRs to align and track; use other mechanisms to evaluate performance.

Cascading and Cadence

Company OKRs flow down: the company has 3–5 objectives with key results; each team has OKRs that support those. Individual OKRs (optional) support team OKRs. Cadence is typically quarterly: set OKRs at the start of the quarter, review progress weekly or in 1:1s, and score at the end. Some teams also set annual OKRs for longer-term direction.

OKR Cadence

  • Quarter start: Set company OKRs, then team OKRs that roll up
  • Weekly: Check progress in team meetings or 1:1s
  • Quarter end: Score key results (e.g., 0–1.0), reflect, set next quarter

Writing Good Key Results

Key Results should be specific, measurable, and time-bound. "Improve customer satisfaction" is vague; "Achieve CSAT score of 4.2+ by end of Q2" is a key result. Avoid key results that are activities ("Launch 5 campaigns") unless the outcome is hard to measure; prefer outcomes ("Generate 500 qualified leads from campaigns").

"If it doesn't have a number, it's not a Key Result. If you can't measure it, you can't manage it."
— Matt Mochary / OKR practice

Scoring and Stretch

At quarter end, score each key result (e.g., 0.0 to 1.0, where 1.0 = 100% achieved). Many companies aim for 0.6–0.7 on average—if you're always at 1.0, your OKRs may be too easy. Stretch goals push the team; just don't make them the basis for punishment. Use scores to learn and set better OKRs next quarter.

Common Mistake

Setting too many OKRs. Focus beats sprawl. 3–5 objectives per company, 2–4 key results per objective. More than that and nothing gets the attention it deserves.

Key Takeaways

  • Objectives are qualitative goals; Key Results are measurable outcomes (usually with a number).
  • Use OKRs for alignment and focus, not as the sole basis for performance evaluation.
  • Cascade from company → team (and optionally individual); review weekly, set quarterly.
  • Write key results that are specific, measurable, and outcome-focused.
  • Keep the list short: 3–5 objectives, 2–4 key results per objective.

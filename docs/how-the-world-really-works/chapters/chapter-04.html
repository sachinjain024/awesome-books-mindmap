How the World Moves

The Invisible Infrastructure of Global Trade

Every morning when you make coffee, you participate in a global supply chain that spans continents. The coffee beans were grown in Ethiopia or Colombia, processed and bagged there or in regional hubs, shipped across oceans on container vessels powered by heavy fuel oil, unloaded at a port, trucked to a warehouse, and delivered to a retailer - all before reaching your cup. And coffee is among the simpler goods in the global trading system.

Modern globalization is built on a foundation of extraordinarily cheap transportation, just-in-time supply chains, and the seamless movement of goods, people, capital, and information across national borders. Smil examines this system with both admiration for its remarkable achievements and concern for its deep fossil fuel dependence and fragility.

The Container Revolution

How Containerization Changed Everything

The modern global economy was made possible not by trade agreements or financial innovation, but by a box: the standardized shipping container, pioneered by Malcolm McLean in 1956.

Before containerization, loading and unloading ships required armies of dock workers and took days. Goods were packed in barrels, crates, and bags of many different sizes, and had to be individually handled. The cost and time of this ‚Äúbreak bulk‚Äù shipping made long-distance trade expensive and slow.

The container changed everything:

  • A container ship can be loaded and unloaded in 24-48 hours using cranes
  • A single large container ship can carry over 20,000 twenty-foot equivalent units (TEUs)
  • The cost of shipping a container across the Pacific has, at times, been as low as a few hundred dollars
  • Shipping costs became so low that it became economical to manufacture goods anywhere in the world

The result: global merchandise trade grew from about $60 billion in 1950 to over $18 trillion by 2019. The world‚Äôs manufacturing geography was completely reorganized around cheap container shipping.

The Fossil Fuel Core of Global Shipping

Ships Run on the Dirtiest Fossil Fuel

The global shipping fleet - approximately 90,000 vessels carrying 80-90% of international trade by volume - runs on heavy fuel oil (bunker fuel), the thick residue left after lighter petroleum products are refined away. It is among the most carbon-intensive and polluting liquid fuels in existence.

Global shipping produces approximately 2.5-3% of global CO‚ÇÇ emissions - more than Germany‚Äôs total annual emissions. And unlike electricity generation, where renewables are rapidly advancing, there is no near-term replacement for heavy fuel oil in large container ships.

Proposed alternatives include:

  • Liquefied natural gas (LNG): Reduces CO‚ÇÇ somewhat but still a fossil fuel; already being deployed
  • Ammonia or methanol: Possible future fuels but infrastructure is minimal and costs high
  • Wind-assisted propulsion: Some ships are installing sails or rotor sails as supplements

None of these alternatives is close to replacing heavy fuel oil at scale for the global fleet.

Aviation: The Other Hard-to-Decarbonize Sector

Aviation accounts for approximately 2.5% of global CO‚ÇÇ emissions - similar to shipping - but its climate impact is higher when high-altitude contrail effects are included. More importantly, aviation is growing rapidly as global middle classes expand their travel.

Unlike ground transportation, where battery-electric vehicles are technically feasible for passenger cars, large commercial aircraft cannot be electrified with current battery technology. A fully loaded Boeing 747 requires about 150,000 liters of jet fuel for a transatlantic flight. The equivalent battery mass would be roughly 30 times the aircraft‚Äôs maximum payload.

Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) made from biofuels or green hydrogen can theoretically replace jet fuel, but current production is a tiny fraction of global aviation demand, and the cost is roughly 3-5 times higher than conventional jet fuel.

The Fragility of Just-in-Time

The COVID-19 pandemic provided an extraordinary demonstration of global supply chain fragility. When demand patterns suddenly shifted and key manufacturing regions shut down, cascading shortages appeared across the global economy - from semiconductors to household goods to medical equipment.

What Supply Chain Disruption Reveals

The pandemic disruptions revealed several structural vulnerabilities:

  1. Geographic concentration: Key components (semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, rare earth elements) are produced in very few locations
  2. Minimal inventory: Just-in-time manufacturing keeps inventory at near-zero, leaving no buffer for disruption
  3. Long lead times: Ocean shipping takes weeks; air freight is expensive; there are few alternatives
  4. Complex interdependencies: A shortage in one component can halt production of products that contain thousands of other components

Smil argues that calls for ‚Äúdeglobalization‚Äù or ‚Äúreshoring‚Äù underestimate how much of modern living standards depends on the international division of labor and cheap transportation. Bringing production home would mean dramatically higher prices and lower variety for consumers.

Reflection

Think about the goods you use daily - your phone, your clothes, your car, your appliances. How many countries were involved in producing them? What would those goods cost if they had to be manufactured entirely within your country? What would be genuinely lost if global supply chains were severely disrupted?

Key Takeaways

  • Containerization was the decisive innovation that enabled modern globalization: standard boxes made shipping so cheap that manufacturing geography was completely reorganized
  • Global shipping accounts for ~2.5-3% of CO‚ÇÇ emissions and runs on heavy fuel oil, the dirtiest petroleum product, with no near-term replacement
  • Commercial aviation cannot be battery-electrified: the energy density of jet fuel is approximately 30 times higher than the best available batteries
  • The COVID-19 pandemic exposed the fragility of just-in-time global supply chains, with geographic concentration and minimal inventory creating cascading shortages
  • Deglobalization would impose significant costs in higher prices and reduced variety - the efficiencies of global trade are real and large
  • Decarbonizing shipping and aviation requires solutions (green ammonia, hydrogen, sustainable aviation fuel) that are currently far too expensive and scarce to deploy at scale

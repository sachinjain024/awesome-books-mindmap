Believe

Part I: Winning the War Within

“In order to convince and inspire others to follow and accomplish a mission, a leader must be a true believer in the mission.” – Jocko Willink

Working with Iraqi Soldiers

This chapter addresses one of the most challenging aspects of SEAL operations in Ramadi: working alongside Iraqi soldiers. Many American troops were skeptical or outright resistant to partnering with Iraqi forces. The Iraqi soldiers were seen as unreliable, poorly trained, and potentially infiltrated by the enemy. Some SEALs questioned why they were risking their lives to train and fight alongside forces they did not trust.

The Order to Partner

When the order came down that all SEAL operations in Ramadi would include Iraqi soldiers as partners, the resistance was fierce. SEAL operators protested. They argued that Iraqi soldiers would compromise their operations, slow them down, and put American lives at risk. Some pointed to incidents where Iraqi soldiers had fled under fire or, worse, turned their weapons on coalition forces.

Jocko faced a critical leadership moment. He could have passed along the order with a shrug, saying “I don’t agree with it either, but orders are orders.” But he knew that approach would guarantee failure. If the leader did not believe in the mission, the team would never execute it effectively.

The Principle: Leaders Must Believe

Before a leader can convince their team to follow, they must first convince themselves. This does not mean blind obedience. It means that the leader must ask questions up the chain of command until they understand the “why” behind every mission. Once the leader genuinely understands and believes in the strategic purpose, they can communicate that belief with conviction.

Understanding the “Why”

  • Ask questions up the chain: If you don’t understand why a decision was made, it is your responsibility to ask. Do not assume the decision is wrong just because you don’t understand it
  • Seek the strategic context: Frontline leaders often lack the broader strategic picture. Understanding the bigger context can transform resistance into belief
  • Find the purpose: Every mission has a purpose. Even missions that seem pointless at the tactical level may serve a critical strategic objective
  • Communicate the “why” down: Once you understand, translate the strategic purpose into terms your team can relate to and believe in
  • Never pass the blame up: Saying “I don’t agree with this but we have to do it” destroys your team’s commitment. If you’ve accepted the mission, own it fully

Jocko’s Realization

Jocko went to his commanding officer and asked blunt questions about the partnering strategy. He learned that the long-term mission in Iraq required Iraqi forces to eventually take over security operations. If American forces did everything themselves, Iraq would never stabilize. The only path to lasting success was building Iraqi capability, and that required working alongside them in combat.

Once Jocko understood this strategic imperative, he genuinely believed in it. He went back to his SEALs and explained the “why” with total conviction. He did not say “headquarters wants us to do this.” He said “this is the only way we win the war.” His belief was contagious. The SEALs stopped resisting and started figuring out how to make the partnership work.

The Danger of Disbelief

When Leaders Don’t Believe

When a leader passes along a directive they clearly don’t believe in, the team senses it immediately. Humans are remarkably skilled at detecting insincerity. A leader who says the right words but conveys doubt through their tone, body language, or level of effort will undermine the mission more effectively than outright opposition.

The most dangerous scenario is a leader who publicly complies but privately undermines. This creates confusion, divided loyalties, and half-hearted execution. If a leader truly cannot believe in a mission after seeking to understand it, they have a duty to push back through the chain of command or, if necessary, to step aside. What they must never do is pretend to support something they actively work against.

Business Application: The New Company Strategy

The Resistant VP

Leif describes a business scenario where a company’s CEO rolled out a major strategic shift. One vice president disagreed with the new direction and made his disagreement known to his team. He told his direct reports, “I don’t agree with this strategy, but we’ve been told to execute it.” The result was predictable: his entire division dragged its feet, resisted the changes, and underperformed.

When Leif coached the VP, he challenged him to go back to the CEO and ask questions until he understood the reasoning behind the strategy. The VP discovered that the CEO had access to market data and competitive intelligence that the VP had not seen. Once the VP understood the full picture, his perspective shifted. He went back to his team and explained the “why” with genuine conviction. His division’s performance immediately improved.

Building Belief in Your Organization

  • Never relay an order you don’t understand. Go up the chain and ask until you do
  • Translate strategy into meaning. Your team doesn’t need corporate jargon; they need to understand how the mission connects to something they care about
  • Share the “why” before the “what.” People execute better when they understand the purpose behind the task
  • Be honest about uncertainty. You don’t have to pretend everything is perfect. You can say “I had questions too, and here’s what I learned”
  • Model belief through action. Your team watches what you do more than what you say. Invest your time and energy in the mission to demonstrate your belief

The Chain of Belief

Belief flows in a chain from the highest levels of leadership down to the frontline operators. If any link in that chain breaks – if any leader fails to truly believe and communicate that belief – the entire chain fails. This is why it is so critical for leaders at every level to seek understanding, achieve genuine buy-in, and then communicate with conviction.

The Belief Cascade

Senior leadership must explain the “why” to their direct reports. Those leaders must internalize it and explain it to their teams. This continues all the way down to the person on the front line who must execute the mission. If the person pulling the trigger or making the sales call doesn’t understand why they’re doing what they’re doing, the mission will fail. Every leader in the chain is responsible for ensuring belief flows through to the next level.

Key Takeaways

  • Leaders must be true believers in the mission before they can inspire others to follow
  • If you don’t understand the “why,” go up the chain and ask until you do
  • Never pass along an order with “I don’t agree but we have to do it” – this guarantees failure
  • Once you understand and believe, communicate the purpose with genuine conviction
  • Belief is contagious – but so is doubt. A leader’s attitude sets the tone for the entire team
  • If you truly cannot support a mission after seeking to understand it, have the integrity to push back or step aside

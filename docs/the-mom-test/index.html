The Mom Test

How to talk to customers and learn if your business is a good idea when everyone is lying to you • by Rob Fitzpatrick

About This Mind Map

This mind map distills Rob Fitzpatrick’s essential guide to customer conversations into an accessible, structured format. The Mom Test is a short, practical book for anyone building a product or starting a business who needs to talk to customers without being misled by polite lies and hollow encouragement.

The core idea is deceptively simple: stop talking about your idea and start asking about their life. Most founders sabotage their own customer research by fishing for compliments instead of searching for truth. Fitzpatrick provides a clear set of rules and techniques for having conversations that actually reveal whether your business idea is worth pursuing.

This mind map walks through all eight chapters, covering the fundamental rules of good customer conversations, how to avoid the most common traps, and how to build a repeatable process for learning from customers as a team. Whether you are a first-time founder or an experienced product manager, these principles will change how you approach customer discovery.

The measure of usefulness of an early customer conversation is whether it gives us concrete facts about our customers' lives and world views. — Rob Fitzpatrick

Core Stoic Principles

  • Talk About Their Life, Not Your Idea: Ask about their problems, workflows, and the specifics of their daily reality instead of pitching your solution.
  • Compliments Are Fool's Gold: People will tell you your idea is great to be polite. Compliments contain zero usable data.
  • Opinions Are Worthless: Only concrete facts about what people have actually done in the past are reliable. Hypotheticals and future promises mean nothing.
  • Ask About Specifics in the Past: Instead of asking what someone would do, ask what they have done. Past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior.
  • Seek Commitment, Not Compliments: A meeting went well only if it ends with a commitment — time, money, reputation, or access to decision-makers.
  • Customer Segments Must Be Specific: You cannot learn anything useful from a vague customer segment. Narrow down until you find consistent patterns.

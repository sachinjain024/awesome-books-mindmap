Shoe Dog

A Memoir by the Creator of Nike • by Phil Knight

About This Book

Shoe Dog is Phil Knight’s raw, unfiltered memoir about building Nike from nothing. It begins in 1962 with a 24-year-old Stanford MBA student who has a single, seemingly absurd idea: import high-quality, low-cost running shoes from Japan and sell them to American athletes. It ends in 1980 with one of the greatest IPOs in business history. In between is one of the most honest accounts of entrepreneurship ever written.

What makes Shoe Dog different from most business memoirs is Knight’s willingness to show the fear, the doubt, and the near-disasters. Nike was weeks from bankruptcy multiple times. Banks dropped them without warning. Their primary supplier tried to destroy them with a lawsuit. The IRS investigated them for fraud. Partners betrayed them. Through it all, Knight and his ragtag team of “shoe dogs” — obsessive, passionate people who lived and breathed shoes — kept running.

This mind map traces the journey decade by decade, from the handshake deal in Kobe that started everything to the iconic Swoosh that changed sports culture forever. Each chapter captures a critical era in Nike’s founding story, distilling the key events, lessons, and the particular brand of stubborn, creative persistence that turned a crazy idea into a global legend.

SHOE DOG
The unlikely story of how Nike changed the world
THE EARLY MILES (1962–1966)
Chapter 1
1962 — The Crazy Idea
The seed of Blue Ribbon Sports
A 24-year-old Phil Knight travels to Japan, walks into Onitsuka Tiger's headquarters in Kobe on a whim, and shakes hands on a deal to import their running shoes to America. The "crazy idea" that would become Nike takes its first breath.
Chapter 2
1963–1964 — First Steps
Bowerman joins, Johnson hustles
The first Tiger shoes arrive. Knight recruits his legendary track coach Bill Bowerman as a co-founder. Jeff Johnson becomes the company's first full-time employee, selling shoes from the trunk of his car at track meets across the country.
Chapter 3
1965–1966 — Cash Flow Crisis
Banks won't lend, but demand keeps growing
Blue Ribbon Sports is growing fast — and nearly dying. Banks refuse to extend credit. Knight discovers the brutal truth that a fast-growing business can be cash-poor while making profit. A Japanese trading company becomes an unlikely lifeline.
BUILDING THE BRAND (1967–1971)
Chapter 4
1967–1968 — Opening Doors
First stores and growing tensions with Onitsuka
Blue Ribbon opens its first retail stores. Jeff Johnson establishes the East Coast operation from Massachusetts. But the relationship with Onitsuka Tiger grows increasingly strained as the Japanese company seeks other American distributors.
Chapter 5
1969–1971 — The Near-Death Year
Banks call loans, Onitsuka sues, Nike is born
The Bank of California calls their loan in a moment of crisis. Onitsuka Tiger moves to end their partnership and sues. In the chaos, Nike is born — a new brand, a new identity, a Swoosh designed for $35, and a name chosen by committee vote.
NIKE IS BORN (1972–1980)
Chapter 6
1972–1974 — The Swoosh Launches
First Olympics with Nike, building brand identity
Nike shoes appear on runners' feet at the 1972 Olympic Trials for the first time. The Swoosh begins its journey from an unknown logo to one of the most recognized symbols in the world. Knight discovers the profound difference between distributing someone else's brand and owning your own.
Chapter 7
1975–1977 — The Waffle Trainer
Bowerman's innovation and explosive growth
Bill Bowerman pours rubber into his wife's waffle iron and invents a sole that changes running forever. The Nike Waffle Trainer becomes a cultural phenomenon. Revenue explodes, organizational chaos follows, and the company discovers the costs of rapid success.
Chapter 8
1978–1980 — Going Public
The IPO and reflection on the journey
Nike becomes a public company. On IPO day, Knight becomes wealthy beyond imagination — and feels mostly numb. He reflects on the team of misfits and dreamers who built something extraordinary, and on what the journey truly meant.

Core Stoic Principles

  • The Crazy Idea: Every great company starts with an idea others think is foolish. Knight's "crazy idea" of importing Japanese running shoes was dismissed by nearly everyone — including his father. Act on the idea anyway.
  • Embrace the Shoe Dog: Knight describes true "shoe dogs" as people who love the shoe business so completely they become consumed by it. This obsession — not strategy or capital — is what drives the excellence that builds enduring companies.
  • Cash Flow Is Life: Nike nearly died multiple times from cash flow problems, not lack of demand. Revenue growth without capital is a trap. Understanding that cash flow trumps profit is a hard-earned business truth that almost ended Nike before it began.
  • Build a Team of Misfits: Knight deliberately hired unconventional people — Jeff Johnson the obsessive collector, Rob Strasser the brawling attorney, Geoff Hollister the runner. Their passion and quirks made them extraordinary. Hire for fire, not just fit.
  • The Relationship with Japan: Nike's early survival depended entirely on its relationships with Japanese manufacturers. Knight's story shows how international partnerships — built on trust and mutual respect — can define a company's entire future.
  • The Long Run: Knight's philosophy is that truly great things take time and sustained effort. Nike's story from 1962 to 1980 is one of perseverance through constant obstacles, doubt, near-bankruptcy, and setback — and choosing to keep going every single time.

Chapter 3: What Can I Contribute?

Practice Two

The effective executive focuses on contribution—on outward results rather than inward effort. They ask "What can I contribute that will significantly affect the performance of the organization?" This focus on contribution is the key to effectiveness.

Focus on Contribution

Most people focus downward—on effort rather than results, on their work rather than its purpose. Effective executives focus outward—on what they can contribute.

"The great majority of executives tend to focus downward. They are occupied with efforts rather than with results. They worry over what the organization and their superiors 'owe' them."
— Peter Drucker

The Central Question

"What can I contribute that will significantly affect the performance and results of the institution I serve?"

Three Areas of Contribution

Drucker identifies three types of contribution every organization needs:

Direct Results

The immediate outputs of your work

Building Values

Reinforcing the organization's commitments

Developing People

Building tomorrow's human resources

An executive who focuses only on direct results neglects the organization's future. All three areas matter.

From Effort to Results

The shift from "What do I do?" to "What can I contribute?" transforms how you think about your role:

Effort vs. Contribution Focus

Effort Focus
  • "I manage 15 people"
  • "I work long hours"
  • "I'm very busy"
  • Focus on status/position
Contribution Focus
  • "I enable these results"
  • "I deliver this value"
  • "I make this difference"
  • Focus on outcomes/impact

Making Specialists Effective

Specialists—experts in narrow fields—face a particular challenge. Their knowledge is useless unless it can be integrated with the knowledge of others.

The Specialist's Dilemma

The specialist must ask: "Who has to use my output for it to become effective? What do they need to know from me? What do I need to know from them?"

The Right Human Relations

Focusing on contribution naturally creates good human relations. When you focus on what you can give rather than what you can get, relationships improve.

Four Requirements of Effective Relations

  1. Communications: Contribution-focused people think about what others need from them
  2. Teamwork: "Who needs my output?" creates natural cooperation
  3. Self-development: You grow when you ask what you can contribute
  4. Development of others: Setting contribution standards helps others grow

Key Takeaways

  • Focus outward on contribution, not inward on effort
  • Ask: "What can I contribute that significantly affects results?"
  • Three contribution areas: direct results, building values, developing people
  • Specialists must translate their knowledge into others' needs
  • Focus on contribution naturally creates good human relations

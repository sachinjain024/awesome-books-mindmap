Chapter 4: Making Strength Productive

Practice Three

The effective executive builds on strength—their own, their colleagues', and their superiors'. They don't focus on weakness. Results come from exploiting strengths, not from shoring up weaknesses to mediocrity.

Staff for Strength

The effective executive staffs for strength. They ask "What can this person do?" not "What can't they do?" Staffing to minimize weakness produces mediocrity.

"The effective executive makes strength productive. He knows that one cannot build on weakness. To achieve results, one has to use all the available strengths."
— Peter Drucker

Lincoln's Lesson

When told that General Grant drank too much, Lincoln replied: "I wish I knew his brand. I'd send a barrel to my other generals." Grant's strength—he won battles—mattered more than his weakness.

The Myth of the "Well-Rounded" Person

Organizations that demand "well-rounded" people with no major weaknesses end up with mediocrity. Strong people have strong weaknesses.

The Weakness Trap

You cannot hire a "hand"—you get the whole person. The person who has no weaknesses is likely to have no strengths either. Average performance comes from average people.

Staffing Approaches

Weakness-Focused
  • "Can they do everything?"
  • Minimize risk of failure
  • Demand well-roundedness
  • Result: Mediocrity
Strength-Focused
  • "What can they do well?"
  • Maximize contribution
  • Accept weaknesses
  • Result: Excellence

Making Your Boss Productive

Effective executives also make their bosses' strengths productive. The boss is a reality—part of your job is to enable their effectiveness.

Managing Up

  • Understand your boss's strengths and work style
  • Present information the way they can absorb it
  • Make them look good at what they do well
  • Don't expect them to change their weaknesses

Making Your Own Strengths Productive

The same principle applies to yourself. Know your strengths and position yourself where they can produce results.

Self-Assessment Questions

  1. What have I done well? What work comes easily to me?
  2. What do I do that others find difficult?
  3. Where can I make the greatest contribution?
  4. What are my limitations that I should work around?

Designing Jobs for Strength

Jobs should be designed to bring out strengths, not to overcome weaknesses. A job that has defeated two or three people in succession is poorly designed.

Job Design Principles

  • Jobs should be demanding—they should challenge
  • Jobs should be big enough to allow growth
  • Jobs should focus on one major strength
  • Restructure jobs that defeat good people

Key Takeaways

  • Staff for strength, not absence of weakness
  • Strong people have strong weaknesses—accept this
  • "Well-rounded" hiring produces mediocre teams
  • Make your boss's strengths productive, not your frustrations
  • Know your own strengths and position yourself to use them
  • If a job defeats multiple people, redesign the job

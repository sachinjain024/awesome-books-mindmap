Chapter 6: Collaborate

Part 4: Drive Results Collaboratively

Getting stuff done collaboratively means avoiding two extremes: being a dictator (deciding everything alone) and being too hands-off (abdicating responsibility). This chapter introduces the "Get Stuff Done" wheel for driving results through collaboration.

The Get Stuff Done Wheel

Great bosses don't just decide or delegate. They create a collaborative process that generates better ideas and stronger buy-in.

The Seven Steps

  1. Listen: Create space for ideas to emerge
  2. Clarify: Sharpen the idea through questions and debate
  3. Debate: Explore perspectives and challenge assumptions
  4. Decide: Make a clear decision (don't let it drag on)
  5. Persuade: Explain the decision and bring skeptics along
  6. Execute: Get it done with clear ownership
  7. Learn: Reflect on results and feed back into listening
"Your job as a leader is not to have all the answers. It's to create an environment where the best ideas surface and win."
— Kim Scott

1. Listen: Create a Culture of Listening

Most bosses listen poorly. They interrupt, finish people's sentences, or mentally prepare their response instead of actually hearing.

How to Really Listen

  • Give quiet time: Not everyone processes thoughts out loud. Give people time to think.
  • Create listening tours: Regularly ask "What's on your mind?" with no agenda
  • Hold office hours: Block time when anyone can drop by
  • Skip the meeting: Walk around and have informal conversations
  • Listen with your whole body: Put away devices, make eye contact, nod

2. Clarify: Sharpen the Idea

Raw ideas are messy. Help people clarify by asking questions, not by taking over and "fixing" their idea.

Clarifying Questions

  • "What problem are we solving?"
  • "Who is this for?"
  • "What would success look like?"
  • "What's the simplest version of this?"
  • "What are we assuming that might not be true?"

3. Debate: Explore Every Angle

Good debate is about finding truth, not winning arguments. Create a culture where people challenge ideas (not people) without fear.

Healthy Debate vs. Unhealthy Debate

Healthy debate:

  • Focuses on ideas, not egos
  • Everyone participates (not dominated by loudest voices)
  • People change their minds when presented with better arguments
  • Ends with stronger ideas

Unhealthy debate:

  • Personal attacks or "winning" at all costs
  • Dominated by senior people or loud voices
  • People dig in and refuse to listen
  • Creates resentment and damaged relationships

4. Decide: Make the Call

After listening, clarifying, and debating, someone needs to decide. Endless debate without decisions kills momentum.

Decision-Making Traps

  • Consensus addiction: Waiting for everyone to agree before deciding (it won't happen)
  • Decision by committee: No one feels ownership, leading to mediocre compromises
  • Too slow: Waiting too long for perfect information
  • Ego-driven: Boss decides without input, undermining the whole process

The fix: Be clear who decides (and when), but separate "who decides" from "who has input."

5. Persuade: Bring People Along

After the decision, you need to persuade people—especially those who disagreed. If people don't understand the "why," they won't execute well.

How to Persuade After a Decision

  • Explain the why: What problem does this solve?
  • Acknowledge dissent: "I know some of you disagree. Here's why we're moving forward anyway."
  • Show you listened: "We considered X, Y, and Z. Here's why we chose A."
  • Be credible: Use emotion AND logic (not just one)
  • Show the data: If you have evidence, share it

6. Execute: Get It Done

Execution requires clear ownership, timelines, and accountability. "We should do this" is not a plan.

Execution Essentials

  • Clear owner: One person owns the outcome (not a committee)
  • Timeline: When will this be done? What are the milestones?
  • Resources: What do they need to succeed?
  • Accountability: How will we track progress?
  • Remove blockers: Your job is to clear the path

7. Learn: Close the Loop

After execution, reflect on what worked and what didn't. Feed learnings back into the next round of listening.

Learning Questions

  • "What went well? Why?"
  • "What didn't go as planned? Why?"
  • "What would we do differently next time?"
  • "What did we learn about our customers/users?"
  • "What assumptions were wrong?"

Celebrate wins AND failures (if you learned something valuable).

Key Takeaways

  • The Get Stuff Done wheel: Listen → Clarify → Debate → Decide → Persuade → Execute → Learn
  • Your job is to create a collaborative process, not have all the answers
  • Listen with intention—create space for ideas to emerge
  • Debate ideas, not people—focus on finding truth
  • Decide clearly and on time—don't let debate drag on endlessly
  • Persuade skeptics by explaining the "why" and acknowledging dissent
  • Execute with clear ownership and accountability
  • Learn from results and feed insights back into listening

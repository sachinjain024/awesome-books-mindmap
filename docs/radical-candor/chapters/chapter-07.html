Chapter 7: Team

Part 4: Drive Results Collaboratively

Building a kick-ass team means hiring right, firing when necessary, promoting carefully, and making tough decisions about team composition. This chapter covers the hardest parts of management: people decisions.

Hiring: Don't Lower the Bar

When you're desperate to fill a role, it's tempting to hire someone "good enough." But one bad hire can damage team culture and morale for years.

The Cost of a Bad Hire

  • Drags down team performance
  • Lowers the bar for future hires ("Well, we hired them...")
  • Demoralizes top performers
  • Forces you to spend time managing them out later
  • Signals you don't care about quality

Hiring Standards

  • Skills: Can they do the job?
  • Trajectory: Are they learning and growing?
  • Culture fit: Do they share your values? (Not "do I want to grab a beer with them")
  • Collaboration: Will they make the team better?
  • The sniff test: Are you genuinely excited to work with them?

If you're unsure, the answer is no.

Firing: Don't Wait Too Long

Most managers wait too long to fire underperformers, hoping they'll improve. This harms the team, the person, and you.

When to Fire Someone

Consider firing if:

  • They're not meeting expectations despite clear feedback and support
  • They violate company values (respect, integrity, etc.)
  • Their presence is damaging team morale or performance
  • You've given them a fair chance to improve and they haven't
  • You're spending all your time managing them instead of the team

Before You Fire: The Three Questions

  1. Have I given Radically Candid feedback? Have you been clear about the problem?
  2. Have I given them time and support to improve? Did they have a real chance?
  3. Is this about performance or fit? Maybe they're great but wrong for this role.

If you can answer yes to the first two and the issue persists, it's time to move on.

"Retaining people who are doing bad work penalizes the people doing excellent work."
— Kim Scott

Promotions: Reward the Right Behaviors

Who you promote sends a signal about what you value. Promote someone who cuts corners, and you've told the team that's what matters.

What to Reward with Promotions

  • Results: They deliver high-quality work consistently
  • Collaboration: They make the team better, not just themselves
  • Growth: They're continuously learning and improving
  • Values: They embody the behaviors you want to see
  • Leadership: Others look to them for guidance

Don't promote based on: tenure alone, politics, or who complains the loudest.

Managing the "Brilliant Jerk"

The brilliant jerk delivers great work but treats people poorly. Many organizations tolerate this, but it's toxic.

The Brilliant Jerk Problem

When you tolerate a brilliant jerk:

  • Top performers leave (they won't tolerate toxicity)
  • Team morale plummets
  • You signal that results matter more than people
  • Others copy the behavior ("If they can act like that, so can I")
  • Your best people become your quietest (they disengage)

The fix: Give them Radically Candid feedback. If they don't change, let them go. Talent is not an excuse for cruelty.

Growing Your Team vs. Shrinking It

Sometimes you need to grow your team; sometimes you need to shrink it. Both decisions require Radical Candor.

When to Grow vs. Shrink

Grow When:
  • Work is piling up and quality is suffering
  • Top performers are burning out
  • You're turning down good opportunities
  • The team is delivering consistently
Shrink When:
  • You have more people than work
  • Performance is inconsistent or declining
  • Team is bloated with politics
  • Budget constraints demand it

Don't grow your team just because it feels like "success." Big teams are harder to manage and slower to move.

The "No Asshole Rule"

Stanford professor Bob Sutton coined the "No Asshole Rule"—don't hire or tolerate people who demean others. Kim Scott agrees: Radical Candor is about challenging people, not being cruel.

Radical Candor ≠ Being Mean

Radical Candor is:

  • Telling someone their work isn't good enough and helping them improve
  • Giving hard feedback because you care about their success
  • Being direct and kind at the same time

Being an asshole is:

  • Belittling people publicly
  • Giving feedback to humiliate, not to help
  • Disrespecting people as human beings

If someone can't tell the difference, they don't belong on your team.

Key Takeaways

  • Don't lower the hiring bar—one bad hire damages the whole team
  • Fire underperformers sooner than feels comfortable (after giving fair feedback)
  • Promotions signal what you value—reward collaboration and values, not just results
  • Don't tolerate brilliant jerks—talent isn't an excuse for cruelty
  • Grow your team when needed, shrink when necessary—size isn't success
  • Enforce the "No Asshole Rule"—Radical Candor is direct, not mean

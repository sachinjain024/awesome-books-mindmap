About This Book

Multipliers is the result of extensive research by Liz Wiseman into why some leaders double the output and capability of their teams while others cut it in half. Drawing on interviews with over 150 leaders across four continents, Wiseman identifies two dramatically different leadership styles and the specific behaviors that distinguish them.

The book is essential reading for anyone in a leadership role, whether you manage a small team or run a large organization. It challenges the assumption that the smartest leader in the room should do all the thinking, and instead reveals how the best leaders create genius in others. The five disciplines provide a concrete framework for shifting from a command-and-control style to one that amplifies collective intelligence.

This mind map distills Wiseman’s research into an accessible format, covering each of the five disciplines with their contrasting behaviors, practical exercises, and the journey of becoming a Multiplier. Whether you suspect you might be an accidental diminisher or want to sharpen your multiplying skills, these chapters offer actionable insights for making everyone around you smarter and more capable.

Core Stoic Principles

  • The 2x Effect: Multipliers get twice the capability from people compared to Diminishers. People report giving 100% of their discretionary effort to Multipliers versus only 50% to Diminishers.
  • Intelligence is not fixed: Multipliers believe intelligence is continually developing. This assumption leads them to invest in people and create conditions where everyone can contribute their fullest.
  • Accidental Diminishers: Most Diminishers don't intend to drain others. Well-meaning leaders often diminish people through habits like always having the answer, rescuing people, or setting the pace themselves.
  • Five Disciplines: Multipliers excel in five roles: Talent Magnet, Liberator, Challenger, Debate Maker, and Investor. Each discipline contrasts with a Diminisher counterpart.
  • Create space for others: The essence of multiplying is restraint. Multipliers use less of their own intelligence so that others can use more of theirs. They lead by asking questions rather than giving answers.
  • Start with one discipline: You don't have to master all five disciplines at once. Choose one area where you are most likely diminishing and work on that first. Small changes create cascading effects.

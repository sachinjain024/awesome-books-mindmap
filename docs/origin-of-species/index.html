On the Origin of Species

By Means of Natural Selection, or the Preservation of Favoured Races in the Struggle for Life • by Charles Darwin

About This Book

This mind map presents Charles Darwin’s revolutionary work that introduced the scientific theory of evolution by natural selection. Published in 1859 after over 20 years of research, this book fundamentally changed our understanding of life on Earth.

Darwin meticulously builds his argument through observations from domestication, nature, geology, and biogeography, addressing objections along the way and culminating in a unified theory of descent with modification.

The book proceeds logically: first showing that variation exists (domestication), then that it exists in nature, then that competition for survival is universal, then that this inevitably leads to selection of beneficial traits. Darwin then addresses difficulties, examines instinct and hybridism, consults the geological record, explores geographical distribution, and finally unites all biology under the principle of common descent.

There is grandeur in this view of life, with its several powers, having been originally breathed into a few forms or into one; and that, whilst this planet has gone cycling on according to the fixed law of gravity, from so simple a beginning endless forms most beautiful and most wonderful have been, and are being, evolved. — Charles Darwin

PART I - VARIATION & SELECTION
Chapter 1
Variation Under Domestication
How breeders create new varieties through artificial selection. Darwin uses pigeons, dogs, and plants to show the immense variability in domesticated species.
Chapter 2
Variation Under Nature
Individual differences exist in wild species too. Darwin examines the blurry line between varieties and species, suggesting they are stages in a continuum.
Chapter 3
Struggle for Existence
More individuals are born than can survive. This competition for limited resources creates the pressure that drives natural selection.
Chapter 4
Natural Selection
The core of Darwin's theory—beneficial variations are preserved, harmful ones eliminated. Includes sexual selection and the divergence of character.
PART II - LAWS & DIFFICULTIES
Chapter 5
Laws of Variation
What causes variation? Darwin explores use and disuse, correlated growth, and how environmental conditions affect organisms across generations.
Chapter 6
Difficulties on Theory
Darwin honestly addresses objections—missing transitional forms, organs of extreme perfection like the eye, and seemingly useless structures.
PART III - INSTINCT & HYBRIDISM
Chapter 7
Instinct
Can complex behaviors evolve? Darwin examines slave-making ants, hive-building bees, and the cuckoo's parasitic instincts as products of natural selection.
Chapter 8
Hybridism
Why are hybrids often sterile? Darwin argues this isn't a special barrier but an incidental result of differences that accumulated during separation.
PART IV - GEOLOGICAL EVIDENCE
Chapter 9
On the Imperfection of the Geological Record
Why don't we find endless transitional fossils? Darwin explains how rare fossilization is and why the geological record is inherently incomplete.
Chapter 10
On the Geological Succession of Organic Beings
The fossil record, despite gaps, shows patterns consistent with descent with modification—gradual change, extinction, and the appearance of related forms.
PART V - GEOGRAPHICAL DISTRIBUTION
Chapter 11
Geographical Distribution
Why are similar environments inhabited by different species? Darwin explores barriers, migration, and how species spread from centers of origin.
Chapter 12
Geographical Distribution (Continued)
Oceanic islands, freshwater species, and alpine floras reveal migration patterns and the profound effects of geological change on species distribution.
PART VI - CLASSIFICATION & CONCLUSION
Chapter 13
Mutual Affinities of Organic Beings
Classification, morphology, embryology, and rudimentary organs all make sense under descent with modification. The "Tree of Life" as the natural system.
Chapter 14
Recapitulation and Conclusion
Darwin summarizes his arguments, addresses remaining difficulties, and envisions the future of biology under the light of evolution.

Core Stoic Principles

  • Variation: Individuals within a species differ from one another in countless ways—no two organisms are exactly alike.
  • Inheritance: Offspring tend to resemble their parents; variations can be passed down through generations.
  • Struggle for Existence: More individuals are born than can survive; resources are limited, creating intense competition.
  • Natural Selection: Individuals with beneficial variations survive and reproduce more successfully, passing traits to offspring.
  • Descent with Modification: Over vast time, small changes accumulate, producing new varieties and ultimately new species.
  • Common Ancestry: All life shares a common origin; species are related through branching descent—the Tree of Life.
  • Gradualism: Evolution proceeds through the accumulation of small, successive changes over immense geological time.
  • Divergence of Character: Natural selection favors variants that differ most from their ancestors and from each other.

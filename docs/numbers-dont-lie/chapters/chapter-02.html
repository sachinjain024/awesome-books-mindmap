The GDP Divide in Numbers

The economic gap between the world‚Äôs richest and poorest countries is almost incomprehensibly large. Luxembourg‚Äôs GDP per capita exceeds $130,000 per year. The Democratic Republic of Congo‚Äôs is around $500. That is a 260-fold difference ‚Äî between people on the same planet, in the same era, subject to the same gravity and breathing the same air.

This gap did not exist in 1800. In the pre-industrial world, standards of living varied considerably across regions, but the gap between the richest and poorest countries was perhaps 4 or 5 to 1. The great divergence ‚Äî the explosion of inequality between nations ‚Äî was caused directly by the Industrial Revolution, which allowed some countries to generate wealth at exponential rates while others were left behind or actively exploited.

What GDP Per Capita Tells Us

GDP per capita is an imperfect but indispensable measure. It captures average material living standards ‚Äî purchasing power, consumption, access to goods and services ‚Äî in a single comparable number. More sophisticated measures like the Human Development Index (HDI) incorporate education and life expectancy, but they correlate strongly with GDP per capita because wealth funds the institutions that improve health and education.

Key benchmarks from recent data:

  • High income (>$13,000): US, Western Europe, Japan, South Korea, Australia
  • Upper middle income ($4,000-$13,000): China, Brazil, Mexico, Russia, Malaysia
  • Lower middle income ($1,000-$4,000): India, Vietnam, Nigeria, Pakistan
  • Low income (<$1,000): Much of sub-Saharan Africa, Haiti, Yemen

Asia‚Äôs Extraordinary Economic Rise

The most remarkable economic story of the past 60 years is the rise of East and Southeast Asia. Japan first demonstrated that a non-Western country could achieve full industrialization. South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore followed. China‚Äôs growth since 1980 has no precedent in human history.

South Korea: A Single Generation

South Korea‚Äôs transformation is particularly striking because it happened within living memory. In 1960, South Korea‚Äôs per capita income was comparable to Ghana‚Äôs ‚Äî approximately $1,200 in today‚Äôs dollars. Today it exceeds $35,000. This 30-fold increase occurred in roughly 50 years, a single human lifetime.

What drove it? Smil points to several factors, but resists simple narratives: heavy investment in education, strategic industrial policy (targeting specific sectors for government support), a high savings rate that funded domestic investment, and integration into global trade networks. The ‚ÄúKorean miracle‚Äù is real, but it required sustained political will, cultural alignment, and fortunate timing in global markets.

China‚Äôs growth since 1980 has been even more dramatic in absolute terms ‚Äî over 800 million people lifted out of poverty, the largest reduction in absolute poverty in human history. China‚Äôs per capita income grew from approximately $200 in 1980 to over $12,000 today ‚Äî a 60-fold increase in 40 years.

Within-Country Inequality

Smil is careful to note that GDP per capita obscures as much as it reveals. The average income of a country says little about how that income is distributed. The United States has the world‚Äôs highest GDP in absolute terms and one of the highest per capita ‚Äî but also one of the highest levels of income inequality among wealthy nations.

The Gini Coefficient

The Gini coefficient measures income inequality on a scale from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (one person owns everything). Key comparisons:

  • Nordic countries (Denmark, Sweden): Gini around 0.28 ‚Äî among the most equal societies
  • Germany, France: Gini around 0.32
  • United States: Gini around 0.41 ‚Äî highly unequal by rich-country standards
  • Brazil, South Africa: Gini above 0.50 ‚Äî among the most unequal large economies

The world‚Äôs richest 1% now own approximately 38% of global wealth. The richest 10% own 76%. These numbers have been increasing in most countries since the 1980s.

What Makes Nations Prosper?

This is one of the most contested questions in all of economics and political science. Smil, characteristically, resists ideology and looks at the data. The correlates of prosperity are clear even if causation is debated:

  • Rule of law and protection of property rights
  • Investment in education, especially at primary and secondary levels
  • Integration into global trade and capital markets
  • Functional institutions that limit corruption and enforce contracts
  • Political stability over multi-decade periods

Key Takeaways

  • The gap between rich and poor countries is historically unprecedented ‚Äî the result of industrial-era divergence, not eternal inequality
  • GDP per capita is an imperfect but essential measure; always ask what it obscures, especially within-country distribution
  • East Asia‚Äôs economic rise is the defining economic story of the past 60 years, demonstrating that rapid development is achievable
  • Within-country inequality is rising in most nations, even as between-country inequality has modestly narrowed
  • Economic development correlates consistently with rule of law, education investment, and institutional quality ‚Äî no shortcuts have been found
  • The most important numbers about a country are per capita, not total ‚Äî always normalize for population size

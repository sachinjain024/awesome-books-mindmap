About This Book

"The Practicing Mind" offers a transformative approach to learning, discipline, and personal growth. Drawing from his experience as a concert piano technician and later as a jazz pianist and golf instructor, Thomas M. Sterner reveals how we can recapture the natural, process-oriented mindset that made learning effortless as children.

The book's central insight is deceptively simple yet profoundly powerful: when we shift our focus from the product we want to achieve to the process of achieving it, something remarkable happens. All pressure, anxiety, and impatience disappear. We stop fighting ourselves and start flowing with the natural rhythm of learning and growth. The practice itself becomes the goal, and mastery emerges as a natural byproduct.

This isn't just a book about practicing an instrument or a sport—it's a comprehensive philosophy for approaching everything in life. Whether you're learning a new skill, building better habits, pursuing career goals, or simply seeking more peace and focus in daily activities, the practicing mind brings clarity, patience, and joy to the journey. Sterner provides practical techniques grounded in both ancient wisdom and modern understanding, making this powerful mindset accessible to anyone willing to shift their perspective from "getting there" to "being here."

This mind map breaks down each chapter's core teachings, making it easy to navigate the key concepts and return to specific practices as you develop your own practicing mind.

Core Stoic Principles

  • Process Over Product: Focus on the process of what you're doing right now, not the end result. When you focus on the process, the product takes care of itself with fluid ease.
  • Stay Present: All your power, energy, and focus exist in the present moment. When you're here now, you're always where you want to be and where you should be.
  • Goals as Compass: Use goals to steer your efforts, not as destinations to rush toward. The goal is the compass, not the buried treasure.
  • Do, Observe, Correct (DOC): The simple three-step cycle that creates improvement—do the activity, observe what happened non-judgmentally, and make corrections for the next attempt.
  • Equanimity Through Non-Judgment: Develop emotional balance by observing without labeling things as good or bad. Non-judgment quiets the internal dialogue and brings peace.
  • The Four S's: Simplify the task, work in Small segments, keep sessions Short, and go Slow. These principles make practice sustainable and effective.

Chapter 6: Difficulties on Theory

Part II: Laws & Difficulties

Darwin was his own toughest critic. In this chapter, he confronts the most serious objections to his theory head-on, demonstrating intellectual honesty that strengthened rather than weakened his argument. By addressing difficulties openly, he showed they were not fatal—and often pointed toward deeper understanding.

The Absence of Transitional Forms

If species evolved gradually from common ancestors, why don't we see endless intermediate forms connecting every species?

The Objection

If evolution is gradual, there should be a continuous series of forms linking every species to its relatives. Instead, species appear distinct, with gaps between them. Where are all the transitional forms?

Darwin's Response

Several factors explain the apparent gaps:

  • Extinction: Transitional forms are eliminated by competition from their more specialized descendants
  • Imperfect record: We only see species that exist now; the transitions happened in the past
  • Local conditions: Intermediates thrived in conditions that no longer exist

The very process of divergence and competition eliminates intermediates, leaving distinct species behind.

Darwin will expand on the fossil record's imperfection in Chapters 9-10, but the key insight is here: we shouldn't expect to find intermediates among living species because the same process that created the divergent forms eliminated the intermediate ones.

Organs of Extreme Perfection

The eye is Darwin's most famous example of this difficulty:

The Objection

Complex organs like the eye are so intricate and perfectly designed that they seem impossible to explain by gradual evolution. What use is half an eye? How could random variation produce such perfection?

"To suppose that the eye, with all its inimitable contrivances for adjusting the focus to different distances, for admitting different amounts of light, and for the correction of spherical and chromatic aberration, could have been formed by natural selection, seems, I freely confess, absurd in the highest possible degree."
— Charles Darwin

But Darwin continues:

Darwin's Response

Yet absurdity is not impossibility. Consider:

  • Gradation exists: Living organisms display every stage of eye complexity, from simple light-sensitive spots to complex image-forming eyes
  • Each stage is useful: A light-sensitive patch helps detect shadows of predators; a cup-shaped eye adds directional information; a pinhole eye produces a crude image; a lens sharpens it
  • Eyes evolved many times: Different animal groups have independently evolved eyes, showing it's not improbable

The Gradation of Eyes

  • Simplest: Pigment spot sensitive to light (flatworms)
  • Cupped: Pigment cup providing direction sensing (some mollusks)
  • Pinhole: Small aperture creating crude image (nautilus)
  • Simple lens: Filled chamber with primitive lens (some snails)
  • Complex lens: Adjustable focus, iris, multiple layers (vertebrates, cephalopods)

Modern research has vindicated Darwin spectacularly: eyes have evolved independently 40-60 times across the animal kingdom, always following similar pathways, suggesting the evolution of complex eyes is not just possible but almost inevitable given enough time.

Organs of Little Apparent Importance

Another difficulty: why would natural selection produce structures that seem trivial or useless?

The Objection

Natural selection only preserves useful variations. So why do organisms have features that seem to serve no purpose? Why do species differ in seemingly arbitrary ways?

Darwin's Response

  • Hidden utility: We often don't understand a structure's function; apparent uselessness may reflect our ignorance
  • Correlated variation: A "useless" trait may be linked to useful ones
  • Changed conditions: What's useless now may have been useful to ancestors
  • Sexual selection: Traits useful for mating may seem arbitrary otherwise

How Could Complex Organs Originate?

Darwin addresses the general problem of how complex organs could arise gradually:

The Key Principle

Complex organs don't arise suddenly; they evolve by modification of pre-existing structures. Each modification must be advantageous in itself, not just as a step toward some future goal. Evolution has no foresight—every intermediate must work.

The Swim Bladder Example

The lung of air-breathing vertebrates evolved from the swim bladder of fish—an organ originally used for buoyancy. The same structure was co-opted for a completely different function. This "co-option" or "exaptation" is a general principle: new complex functions often arise by modifying structures that evolved for other purposes.

Modes of Transition

How can organs change function? Darwin shows that transitional stages are not as problematic as they first appear:

Functional Transitions

  • Flying fish: Fins that aid both swimming and gliding
  • Climbing fish: Fins modified for both water and land locomotion
  • Water birds: Wings used for swimming, flying, and display

The same organ can serve multiple functions, allowing gradual shift from one primary use to another.

Distinct Species with Similar Habits

Why aren't all species that live similarly constructed similarly?

Darwin's Response

Evolution works with available materials. Different lineages may adapt to similar environments in different ways, depending on their ancestral starting points. The duck's webbed feet and the coot's lobed toes both work for swimming—different solutions to the same problem, reflecting different ancestry.

Key Takeaways

  • Darwin honestly confronted the strongest objections to his theory
  • Missing transitional forms are explained by extinction and competition
  • Complex organs like the eye show gradation in living species—every stage is functional
  • Apparently useless structures may have hidden functions, be correlated with useful traits, or be vestiges
  • Complex organs evolve by modification of pre-existing structures (co-option)
  • Organs can serve multiple functions, allowing gradual transitions
  • Different lineages may solve the same problem differently based on their ancestry
